A cheat sheet for newcomers and a memory aid for regulars. Each card covers one liturgical moment: what's going on, what to do, and why. Organized by service type.

The Divine Liturgy

Opening Blessing
"Blessed is the Kingdom…"
Right now… The priest is declaring the destination of the entire Liturgy — the Kingdom of God.
You should… Stand. Cross yourself at "Amen." This is the beginning.
This means… The Liturgy starts not with a greeting but with a blessing of the Kingdom itself. Everything that follows takes place within that reality.
Great Litany
"In peace let us pray to the Lord…"
Right now… The deacon is leading a series of prayers for the world, the Church, and the community.
You should… Stand. Respond "Lord, have mercy" after each petition (or listen and pray silently).
This means… The Church prays for the whole world before itself — from universal peace down to travelers and the sick.
Antiphons
"Bless the Lord, O my soul…"
Right now… Psalm verses are being sung antiphonally, tracing salvation history from the Old Testament through the Incarnation.
You should… Stand. Listen or sing along if you know the melody. The "Only-begotten Son" hymn comes between the first two antiphons.
This means… The Church is rehearsing the story of God's saving work — creation, prophecy, then Christ.
Small Entrance
"Wisdom! Let us attend!"
Right now… The priest is carrying the Gospel book through the church in procession.
You should… Stand. Bow as the Gospel passes you. Some touch the priest's vestment.
This means… Christ entering among His people. The Gospel book is an icon of Christ's presence.
Trisagion
"Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal…"
Right now… The congregation is singing one of the oldest hymns in Christianity, joining the song of the angels.
You should… Stand. Sing along — the melody is simple. Cross yourself with each repetition (three times).
This means… The Trisagion echoes Isaiah's vision of the seraphim. The congregation does what the angels do.
Epistle Reading
Prokeimenon, then the reading
Right now… A reader is chanting a passage from the apostolic letters or Acts.
You should… You may sit (practice varies by parish). Listen attentively.
This means… The apostles' teaching, addressed to this assembly, today.
Gospel Reading
"Wisdom! Let us attend! Let us listen to the Holy Gospel."
Right now… The priest or deacon is reading the Gospel — the words of Christ Himself.
You should… Stand. Face the reader. Do not sit during the Gospel.
This means… Christ is speaking to His Church. Standing shows that we are ready to obey.
Cherubic Hymn
"Let us who mystically represent the Cherubim…"
Right now… The choir is singing the hymn that prepares for the Great Entrance. The congregation is being asked to set aside earthly concerns.
You should… Stand still. Bow your head. This is the most contemplative moment in the service.
This means… The congregation is joining the angelic worship — "mystically representing" the Cherubim themselves.
Great Entrance
"May the Lord God remember all of you in His Kingdom…"
Right now… The priest is carrying the bread and wine in procession through the church to the altar.
You should… Bow deeply. Some bow from the waist. Do not reach out to touch the gifts. Whisper names of loved ones if you wish.
This means… The gifts — and the lives of everyone who brought them — are being offered to God. The King is arriving.
The Creed
"I believe in one God, the Father Almighty…"
Right now… The entire congregation is reciting the Nicene Creed — the baptismal confession of faith.
You should… Stand. Recite along if you can. This is the faith the Eucharist rests on.
This means… The community declares together what it believes before approaching the mystery.
Epiklesis (Consecration)
"Send down Your Holy Spirit upon us and upon these gifts…"
Right now… The priest is invoking the Holy Spirit to transform the bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ.
You should… Kneel or bow deeply. This is the most sacred moment of the entire Liturgy.
This means… The Spirit descends. The bread and wine become what they were offered to become. Heaven and earth meet.
Lord's Prayer
"Our Father, who art in heaven…"
Right now… The congregation is praying the prayer Jesus taught, together.
You should… Stand. Pray along — everyone knows this one. Bow your head.
This means… "Daily bread" here means the Eucharist. Calling God "Father" is a privilege granted to the baptized.
"The Holy Things for the Holy"
"The holy things for the holy!"
Right now… The priest is elevating the consecrated Lamb and declaring that these gifts are for those who are holy.
You should… Bow. The congregation responds: "One is holy, one is Lord — Jesus Christ."
This means… No one approaches Communion on their own worthiness. Only Christ is holy. We receive by His mercy.
Communion
People approach the chalice
Right now… The faithful are receiving the Body and Blood of Christ together, by spoon from the chalice.
You should… If communing: arms crossed over chest, head tilted slightly back, mouth open. State your baptismal name. If not communing: remain standing and pray quietly.
This means… The faithful receive the risen Christ — Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity — for the forgiveness of sins and eternal life.
"We Have Seen the True Light"
"We have seen the true light…"
Right now… The post-Communion hymn — a summary of everything that just happened.
You should… Stand. Sing if you can. This is a moment of quiet joy.
This means… Present tense: "We have seen." Not "we hope to see." The Liturgy was a real encounter.
Dismissal & Antidoron
"Let us go forth in peace…"
Right now… The Liturgy is ending. The priest gives the final blessing and distributes antidoron (blessed bread).
You should… Approach, kiss the cross, receive a piece of bread. Everyone may receive antidoron.
This means… You are sent back into the world carrying what the Liturgy gave you. The antidoron is a sign of fellowship for all.

Vespers

Opening Psalm (Psalm 103/104)
"Bless the Lord, O my soul! O Lord my God, You are very great…"
Right now… The psalm of creation is being chanted. The Royal Doors are open; incense fills the church.
You should… Stand. Take in the scene — doors open, incense rising, the creation psalm sung as the liturgical day begins.
This means… Vespers begins with creation because the liturgical day begins at sunset, mirroring Genesis: "And there was evening, and there was morning."
"Lord, I Have Cried"
"Lord, I have cried unto Thee, hear me…" (Psalm 140/141)
Right now… The key psalms of Vespers are being chanted, with stichera (hymn verses) inserted between psalm verses.
You should… Stand. The stichera carry the theme of the day or feast — listen for them.
This means… The psalms express longing for God; the stichera connect that longing to the specific feast or saint being celebrated.
Entrance & "O Gladsome Light"
"O Gladsome Light of the holy glory of the immortal Father…"
Right now… The priest processes with the censer (or Gospel book on great feasts). The ancient hymn Phos Hilaron is sung.
You should… Stand. Bow during the entrance. This hymn dates to at least the 3rd century — you are singing something Christians have sung for 1,700+ years.
This means… As the sun sets, the Church turns to Christ — the true, unfading Light. The transition from natural to liturgical light.
Prokeimenon
A psalm verse chanted responsively
Right now… A psalm verse is being chanted that sets the theme for the day. On Saturday evenings, it's always the resurrection prokeimenon.
You should… Stand. Listen — this verse encapsulates the theological focus of the day or feast.
This means… The prokeimenon is a scriptural lens through which the Church reads the day.
Litiya & Artoklasia
Procession to the narthex, blessing of loaves
Right now… On great feasts: the priest processes to the narthex for extended intercessions, then blesses five loaves, wheat, wine, and oil.
You should… Follow if there's a procession. Stand for the blessing. You'll receive a piece of the blessed bread afterward.
This means… The Church feeds its people — a literal echo of Christ's multiplication of loaves. The oil is used to anoint the faithful.
"Now You Let Your Servant Depart"
"Now You let Your servant depart in peace…" (Simeon's Song, Luke 2:29)
Right now… The Song of Simeon, who held the infant Christ in the Temple, is being sung near the end of Vespers.
You should… Stand. This is near the close of the service — a moment of quiet peace.
This means… Like Simeon, we have seen God's salvation. The day can end in peace.

Etiquette & Posture

There is no single "right" way — practices vary by parish and cultural tradition. These are general guidelines for Byzantine Catholic and Orthodox churches.

When to Stand
Default: Standing is the normative posture. Most of the Liturgy is done standing.
Always stand for: The Gospel reading, the Great Entrance, the Creed, the Anaphora/Epiklesis, the Lord's Prayer, Communion, and the Dismissal.
Note: If you can't stand for health reasons, sit. No one will judge you. The tradition values the intention.
When to Sit
You may sit during: The Epistle reading (parish-dependent), the homily/sermon, and extended hymn-singing during Matins.
Watch others: If the people around you are sitting, it's probably fine to sit. If they're standing, stand.
Sign of the Cross
How: Right hand — thumb, index, and middle fingers joined (Trinity); ring and pinky to palm (two natures of Christ). Touch forehead, chest, right shoulder, then left shoulder (opposite of the Latin practice).
When: At every "Father, Son, and Holy Spirit," at "Amen" after blessings, before and after venerating an icon, and instinctively whenever you feel moved. Frequency varies — some people cross themselves constantly.
Bowing
Small bow (head): At the name of Christ, the Theotokos, or the saint of the day. At "Lord, have mercy." When the priest blesses the congregation.
Deep bow (waist): During the Great Entrance. At the Epiklesis. At "The holy things for the holy." During the Lenten prayer of St. Ephrem.
Prostrations
What: A full prostration means kneeling and touching your forehead to the floor, then standing again.
When: Primarily during Great Lent — at the Prayer of St. Ephrem (with its 12+ prostrations) and at the Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts. Generally not done on Sundays or from Pascha to Pentecost.
If unsure: Follow the regulars. If nobody's prostrating, don't.
Venerating Icons
How: Approach the icon. Cross yourself twice. Kiss the icon (hands or feet of Christ; hands of the Theotokos; hand of a saint). Cross yourself a third time. Step away.
When: Upon arriving at church, before the Liturgy begins. Also after receiving antidoron or at special veneration moments (feast days, after the Gospel on certain days).
Where to kiss: Never on the face. Kiss the hand, foot, or Gospel book depicted in the icon.
Arriving & Leaving
Arriving: Arrive before "Blessed is the Kingdom" if possible. Venerate the icons near the entrance. Find a spot and stand quietly. Late arrival is common — don't feel embarrassed, but enter during a hymn, not during the Gospel or Epiklesis.
Leaving: Stay for the dismissal and antidoron if you can. Leaving during Communion is considered discourteous. If you must leave early, slip out during a hymn.
If You're Not Orthodox/Byzantine Catholic
Communion: Holy Communion is reserved for Catholics in a state of grace who have observed the Eucharistic fast. Orthodox Christians may receive under certain conditions — speak with the priest. Visitors of other traditions should not receive Communion, but you are welcome to participate in everything else and to receive antidoron (blessed bread) at the dismissal.
Antidoron: Everyone may receive the blessed bread at the end. Approach and receive it — it's a gift of fellowship.
Everything else: Stand, sit, sing along, make the sign of the cross — or don't. Nobody will quiz you. Just be present and respectful.
