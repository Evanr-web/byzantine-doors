The Daily Cycle of Prayer

The Divine Liturgy does not exist in isolation. It is surrounded by a daily cycle of services inherited from the monastic tradition. In parishes, most of these are celebrated only on major feast days or during Lent. In monasteries, they are the pulse of every day.

Vespers

The evening service. Begins the liturgical day — in Byzantine reckoning, the day starts at sunset, not midnight. Themes of creation, light, and thanksgiving. The hymn Phos Hilaron ("O Gladsome Light") is among the oldest Christian hymns still in use.

Matins

The morning service. Longer and richer than Vespers. Themes of resurrection and light breaking into darkness. The Canon, a complex poetic composition of nine odes, forms its centerpiece. On Sundays, the Resurrection Gospel is read.

The Hours

Short services marking the first, third, sixth, and ninth hours (roughly 6 AM, 9 AM, noon, 3 PM). Psalms, troparia, and prayers. They punctuate the day with remembrance of Christ's Passion.

Compline

The last prayer before sleep. Themes of repentance, trust, and surrender. The Great Compline of Lent includes the penitential Canon of St. Andrew of Crete.

Four Hymns Worth Knowing

The Liturgy is almost entirely sung. No instruments — human voices only. These four recur so frequently that learning them is the fastest way to feel at home:

The Trisagion
"Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us."
Sung three times, invoking the Trinity. One of the oldest hymns in Christianity, attested from the fifth century.
The Cherubic Hymn
"Let us who mystically represent the Cherubim, and who sing the thrice-holy hymn to the life-creating Trinity, now lay aside all earthly cares…"
Accompanies the Great Entrance. The congregation sets aside everything to join the angelic worship.
"We Have Seen the True Light"
"We have seen the true light. We have received the heavenly Spirit. We have found the true faith, worshipping the undivided Trinity, who has saved us."
Sung after Communion. The entire Liturgy summarized in four lines.
"It Is Truly Right"
"It is truly right to bless you, O Theotokos, ever blessed and most pure, and the Mother of our God."
Sung during the anaphora. Its place at the heart of the Eucharistic prayer reflects the connection between the Incarnation and the Eucharist.

Further Reading

Alexander Schmemann's For the Life of the World is the essential starting point: short, luminous, and transformative. His Introduction to Liturgical Theology goes deeper into structure and history. For a walk-through of the Liturgy itself, see Hugh Wybrew's The Orthodox Liturgy or the Divine Liturgy: An Anthology for Worship (Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church). The catechism Christ Our Pascha covers sacraments and liturgical life in §§ 432–620.

English hymn texts are sourced from publicly available translations and may differ from those used in your parish. This site is not an official publication of any eparchy, diocese, or church body.
