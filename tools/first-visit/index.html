Practical Guide

Your First Visit to a Byzantine Parish

Everything you need to know before, during, and after your first Divine Liturgy.

No preparation required. No dress code. No prerequisite beliefs. But if knowing what to expect helps settle the nerves, keep reading.

1

Before You Go

Search "Byzantine liturgy" plus your city. Sunday times vary, usually between 9:00 and 11:00 AM. Arrive five to ten minutes early. That's it.

What to wear: Business casual is a safe bet. Some older parishioners may wear head coverings. Visitors are not expected to.
2

When You Walk In

Beeswax and incense. That's the first thing. The church will be dimmer than expected. Look for the icon of Christ to the right of the main doors, the Theotokos (Mary) to the left.

What you'll see: A wall of icons (the iconostasis) across the front. Candle stands. Perhaps older parishioners crossing themselves and bowing. The space is alive before the service starts.
3

During the Liturgy

Almost everything is sung. The congregation stands for most of the service. Sitting is fine if needed. Most parishes provide handouts with the responses.

Do not worry about following along perfectly. No one expects that.

What you'll hear: Chanting, sometimes in English, sometimes in a liturgical language. No instruments. Only human voices.
4

Holy Communion

Holy Communion is reserved for Catholics in a state of grace who have observed the Eucharistic fast (typically one hour before receiving). Orthodox Christians may receive under certain conditions — speak with the priest beforehand. Visitors of other traditions should not approach for Communion.

Everyone is welcome to receive antidoron (blessed bread) at the dismissal — approach the priest, venerate the cross, and receive a piece. If you would like a blessing during Communion, cross your arms over your chest and the priest will bless you.

5

After the Liturgy

Go to coffee hour. This matters. Eastern Christians are famously hospitable. Someone will hand you a plate of food within five minutes. Possibly sooner.

What to expect: Coffee, conversation, homemade food. If someone asks "is this your first time?" — that's an invitation, not an interrogation.

Catechetical and parish resources for the Byzantine Christian tradition
Byzantine Doors is an independent educational project created by a layperson to help English-speaking seekers and families discover the Byzantine Christian tradition. Theological content draws primarily from Christ Our Pascha, the catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church. English hymn texts are sourced from publicly available translations and may differ from those used in your parish. This site is not an official publication of any eparchy, diocese, or church body.
