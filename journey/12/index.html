Fasting and Feasting

The rhythm of the body — why Eastern Christians fast, and why the feast that follows changes everything.

Western Christianity has largely forgotten how to fast. The Roman Catholic Church, which once maintained a rigorous fasting discipline, has reduced its requirements to two obligatory fast days per year: Ash Wednesday and Good Friday. Most Protestants have abandoned the practice altogether. Ask the average Western Christian about fasting, and the answer is likely something vague about "giving something up for Lent." Perhaps chocolate, or social media, or beer.

The Eastern Church views these things with gentle bewilderment.

In the East, fasting is not a seasonal novelty. It is a way of life. Eastern Christians fast, on average, nearly half the days of the year. This is not an exaggeration. Between the four major fasting seasons and the weekly Wednesday-Friday fast, approximately 180 to 200 days per year involve some form of dietary discipline. The body is not an afterthought in Eastern spirituality. It is a participant, an ally, and sometimes a battleground.

The Four Fasting Seasons

The Eastern Church observes four extended fasting periods, each preparing the faithful for a great feast:

The Great Fast (Great Lent) — seven weeks before Pascha, the most intense fasting period. The first week is the strictest: some monasteries observe a complete fast on Monday and Tuesday, followed by extremely austere meals the rest of the week. The standard lay discipline involves abstaining from meat, dairy, eggs, fish, wine, and oil. Essentially a vegan diet for the entire period. This is the crucible of the Eastern Christian year.

The Apostles' Fast — begins the Monday after All Saints Sunday (one week after Pentecost) and continues until the feast of Saints Peter and Paul on June 29. Its length varies depending on the date of Pascha, from as short as a week to as long as six weeks.

The Dormition Fast — August 1 through 14, preparing for the feast of the Dormition (Falling Asleep) of the Theotokos on August 15. Despite its brevity, this fast is kept with the same strictness as Great Lent.

The Nativity Fast (Advent) — November 15 through December 24, preparing for the Nativity of Christ. Less strict than Great Lent (fish is permitted on most days) but still a genuine period of discipline and anticipation.

Fasting gives birth to prophets and strengthens the powerful. Fasting makes lawmakers wise. It is a safeguard of the soul, a steadfast companion to the body, a weapon for the valiant, a gymnasium for athletes of the spirit.
— St. Basil the Great, On Fasting, 4th century

Wednesday and Friday

Beyond the four seasons, Eastern Christians fast every Wednesday and Friday throughout the year (with a few exceptions during feast weeks). Wednesday commemorates Judas's betrayal of Christ. Friday commemorates the Crucifixion. The weekly fast follows the same basic discipline as the longer fasts: no meat, dairy, or eggs, and sometimes no fish, wine, or oil, depending on the strictness observed.

This weekly rhythm creates a constant alternation between discipline and freedom, hunger and satisfaction, cross and resurrection. The body never forgets that it is living inside a story. Wednesday: betrayal. Friday: death. Sunday: resurrection.

The gospel is written not just in books but in the stomach.

Fasting as Medicine

The Eastern understanding of fasting is therapeutic, not juridical. You do not fast to earn God's favor or to pay for sins. You fast because you are sick and fasting is part of the cure. The "sickness" in question is the disordered attachment of the will to bodily pleasures, what the Fathers called the passions. The goal is not the suppression of desire but its reordering: learning to desire the right things, in the right measure, at the right time.

St. John Chrysostom insisted that fasting from food without fasting from sin is worthless. A person who keeps a perfect vegan Lent but gossips viciously throughout has missed the point entirely. Conversely, a person whose health prevents strict fasting but who practices patience, generosity, and forgiveness during Lent is truly fasting.

The rules are a framework, not a cage. Pregnant women, the elderly, the ill, and children are expected to modify their fast. The spiritual father or priest helps each person calibrate the discipline to their actual condition. Growth, not performance.

The Feast That Follows

And then comes the feast.

This is the part that modern culture, with its year-round access to every food and pleasure, cannot replicate. When you have fasted genuinely, the feast that breaks the fast is transfigured. Not merely enjoyable. Revelatory.

The Paschal feast is the supreme example. After seven weeks of increasing austerity, after the long services of Holy Week, after the solemn silence of Great Saturday, the Paschal Vigil erupts into light and song at midnight. And then the fast is broken. The table is loaded with everything that has been forbidden: lamb, cheese, eggs (often the beautiful red-dyed eggs of the Paschal tradition), rich pastries, wine. The contrast with the preceding weeks is overwhelming. The body, which has been disciplined and emptied, now receives abundance, and the abundance means something. It is resurrection made tangible.

The table is full-laden; feast ye all sumptuously. The calf is fatted; let no one go away hungry. All of you, enjoy the feast of faith; all of you, receive the riches of goodness.
— St. John Chrysostom, Paschal Homily, 4th century

The same principle operates at every feast throughout the year, though in smaller measure. The Dormition Fast gives way to the joy of August 15. The Nativity Fast gives way to the twelve days of Christmas celebration. Even the weekly fast gives way to the freedom of Saturday and Sunday. Constant rhythm: emptying and filling, cross and resurrection, death and life.

The Connection Between Body and Spirit

Eastern Christianity refuses to separate the body from the spirit. The human person is a unity, psychosomatic, in the Fathers' language, and what affects one dimension affects all the others. Fasting disciplines the body, but its effects are spiritual: greater clarity of mind, heightened sensitivity to prayer, a loosening of the passions' grip. Feasting nourishes the body, but its meaning is spiritual: gratitude, communion, celebration of God's goodness.

Fasting without prayer is just dieting. Prayer without fasting can become disembodied. Together, they form a complete discipline, a training of the whole person, body and soul, for the life of the age to come.

Modern Western culture treats the body as either an object to be perfected (fitness culture) or an appetite to be indulged (consumer culture). The Eastern tradition offers a third way: the body as a temple. Not to be punished, not to be pampered, but to be sanctified. Fasting is the tool. Feasting is the reward. And the rhythm between them is the heartbeat of the Christian life.

The next step introduces the men and women who have walked this path before: the saints, the cloud of witnesses who prove that theosis is not a theory but a lived reality.
