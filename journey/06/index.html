Step 06

Salvation as Healing

Theosis — the audacious claim that the goal of human life is to become God by grace.

Home › Journey › Salvation as Healing

Read time 7 min read
Key terms

Ask a Western Christian "What does salvation mean?" and the answer will often involve forgiveness. Christ died for your sins. The debt has been paid. You are pardoned. There is deep truth in this, but it is not the whole picture, and it is not where Eastern Christianity places the emphasis. Ask a Byzantine Christian the same question, and you are likely to hear something far more startling.

Salvation means becoming God.

Not metaphorically. Not loosely. The Eastern theological tradition has a precise word for this: theosis, deification, divinization, the real participation of human beings in the divine nature. It is, by any measure, the most audacious claim in all of Christian theology. And it is the beating heart of Eastern Christianity.

God became human so that humans might become God.
— St. Athanasius of Alexandria, On the Incarnation, 4th century

Not a Legal Transaction

To understand theosis, it helps to understand what Eastern Christianity is not saying about salvation.

In the Western tradition, particularly after the Reformation, salvation came to be understood primarily in legal or forensic terms. Humanity stands guilty before a just God. Christ, as a substitute, takes the punishment deserved. The debt is paid, the ledger is balanced, and the sinner is declared righteous. This is the framework of penal substitutionary atonement, and it has been enormously influential.

Eastern Christianity never developed this framework. Not because it denies the reality of sin, or the significance of the Cross, or the need for repentance. It affirms all of these. But the dominant metaphor is different. The East does not start with a courtroom. It starts with a hospital.

As the previous step explored, the Fall is understood as sickness, not primarily as guilt. If the problem is illness, then the solution is not acquittal but healing. And that healing has a destination. It does not merely restore humanity to some neutral state of "not sick." It transforms humanity into something it was always meant to become.

The Hospital of the Church

The Church Fathers consistently describe the Church not as a courtroom but as a hospital for souls. The priest is a physician. The sacraments are medicine. Sin is a wound or a disease. Confession is diagnosis. Holy Unction, the anointing of the sick with blessed oil, is administered not only for physical illness but for the healing of the whole person, body and soul together.

This metaphor runs through every aspect of Byzantine church life. When a person goes to confession, the priest does not sit behind a screen pronouncing legal sentences. He stands beside the penitent, both of them facing an icon of Christ. The prayer of absolution asks God to "heal" the one who confesses.

Even the penances assigned after confession, fasting, additional prayer, prostrations, are not punishments. They are prescriptions. A doctor does not punish a patient for being sick; the doctor prescribes a treatment. That is exactly how Eastern Christianity understands the spiritual life.

What Theosis Actually Means

So what does it mean to "become God"?

The Eastern Fathers were careful to distinguish between God's essence and God's energies. God's essence, what God is in Himself, remains forever beyond human comprehension or participation. No creature can become God in that sense. But God's energies, His grace, His light, His love, His life, are genuinely shared with human beings. Theosis is participation in the divine energies, not absorption into the divine essence.

St. Maximus the Confessor, writing in the seventh century, put it this way: in theosis, the human person becomes "everything that God is, except at the level of being." Sharing God's life, God's love, God's creativity, while remaining a creature. Not dissolving into God. Permeated by God, as iron placed in fire becomes incandescent with heat and light while remaining iron.

The human person becomes by grace everything that God is by nature — without any confusion of natures.
— St. Maximus the Confessor, Ambiguum 7, 7th century

Gregory Palamas and the Light of Tabor

In the fourteenth century, St. Gregory Palamas defended the reality of theosis against those who argued it was merely a metaphor. Palamas pointed to the Transfiguration, the moment on Mount Tabor when Christ's face shone like the sun and His garments became dazzling white. The light the disciples saw, Palamas argued, was not created light. It was the uncreated energy of God Himself, the same light that shines in the saints, the same light available to every baptized Christian through prayer, sacrament, and ascetic struggle.

This was not abstract philosophy for Palamas. He was a monk of Mount Athos who practiced the Jesus Prayer, "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner," and he testified that this prayer, practiced with humility and persistence, opened the human person to genuine encounter with the divine light. The monks of Athos, he claimed, were not imagining things. They were experiencing theosis.

What Does Theosis Look Like in Daily Life?

This may sound exalted and remote, the province of monks on mountains, not ordinary people in apartments. The Eastern tradition insists otherwise. Theosis is the vocation of every baptized Christian. It looks different in different lives, but its marks are recognizable:

Patience that is not performance. A person growing in theosis does not merely control their temper. The anger itself begins to dissolve, replaced by something genuinely peaceful.

Compassion that is not calculated. The love of neighbor becomes instinctive. The saints describe reaching a state where they genuinely cannot see another person suffer without feeling it as their own pain.

Prayer that becomes breath. The Jesus Prayer, practiced over years, moves from the lips to the mind to the heart. A continuous, quiet awareness of God's presence that persists even during ordinary tasks. The Way of a Pilgrim, an anonymous nineteenth-century Russian classic, is the best introduction to this experience.

Freedom from passions. The "passions" in Eastern spirituality, anger, lust, greed, vanity, despair, are not destroyed but transformed. They are the raw material of virtue. Anger becomes righteous zeal. Desire becomes love of God. The energy remains; its direction changes.

The Sacraments as Medicine

The primary vehicle of theosis is not moral effort alone. It is the life of the Church, particularly the sacraments. Baptism is rebirth. Chrismation, the Eastern equivalent of Confirmation, seals the new Christian with the gift of the Holy Spirit. The Eucharist is not merely a memorial but a real encounter with the risen Christ, "the medicine of immortality," as St. Ignatius of Antioch called it in the first century.

Each sacrament addresses a different dimension of the human person's journey toward God. Together, they form a comprehensive therapy, not because the Church thinks grace is mechanical, but because God, who made human beings as embodied creatures, heals them through embodied means.

The next step introduces the One who makes all of this possible: the Holy Spirit, the Lord and Giver of Life, who dwells within each Christian and draws them, step by step, into the life of God.

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on Salvation and Theosis
▾

Christ Our Pascha §§ 218–232 treats salvation as deification — the transformation of the human person by grace. "The goal of human life is theosis, the union of the human person with God" (§218). The catechism presents the sacramental and ascetic life of the Church as the concrete means by which this transformation occurs: baptism, Eucharist, prayer, and fasting are not obligations but medicine for the journey toward God.

Practice

Theosis is not an idea to agree with. It is a life to live. Start where the Fathers started: with the Jesus Prayer. Set aside five minutes today. Sit quietly. Breathe slowly. Repeat: Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner. Do not analyze it. Just pray it. Tomorrow, do it again.
