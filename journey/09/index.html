Step 09

The Mysteries

The seven sacraments of the East — where heaven meets earth in water, oil, bread, and wine.

The Western Church calls them sacraments, sacred signs that effect what they signify. The Eastern Church calls them Mysteries, and the difference in terminology is not accidental. A sacrament, in the popular Western imagination, can sometimes feel like a transaction: the right words are said, the right matter is used, and grace is dispensed. A Mystery is an encounter. Something experienced, entered into, and never fully comprehended. The Eastern Church does not claim to understand how bread becomes the Body of Christ. It simply confesses that it does, falls to its knees, and gives thanks.

Seven Mysteries: Baptism, Chrismation, Eucharist, Confession, Marriage, Holy Orders, and Holy Unction. Each one addresses a different dimension of human life. Together, they form a comprehensive therapy for the whole person, body, soul, and spirit, from birth to death and beyond.

The Three Mysteries of Initiation

Here is something that surprises many Western Christians: in the Eastern tradition, Baptism, Chrismation, and the Eucharist are administered together, in a single service, even to infants. A baby baptized on a Sunday morning will, within the same hour, be chrismated with holy oil and receive Communion, the Body and Blood of Christ on a tiny golden spoon.

The logic is simple. If Baptism makes you a member of the Body of Christ, and the Eucharist is the food of that Body, then why withhold the food from a new member? The East does not require understanding as a prerequisite for grace. An infant does not understand mother's milk either, but no one withholds it on that account.

You were led to the sacred pool of divine Baptism, as Christ was led from the Cross to the Tomb. And each of you was asked whether you believed. And you confessed your faith — and descended three times into the water, and rose again.
— St. John Chrysostom, Baptismal Instructions, 4th century

Baptism

Baptism is death and resurrection. The candidate, infant or adult, is immersed three times in blessed water, in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. Triple immersion as burial: going down into the waters of death with Christ. The rising is a resurrection: emerging into new life. The white garment placed on the newly baptized signifies the "new self" put on in Christ.

Full immersion is the Eastern norm, and it is considered theologically important. The symbolism of burial and rising is lost if water is merely poured. The water itself has been blessed by the epiklesis, the invocation of the Holy Spirit, making it a new Jordan, a new Red Sea, a passage from slavery to freedom.

Chrismation

Chrismation immediately follows Baptism. The priest anoints the newly baptized with holy chrism, a specially consecrated oil blessed by the bishop, making the sign of the cross on the forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, chest, hands, and feet. At each anointing: "The seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit."

This is the Eastern equivalent of Confirmation, but it is not delayed until adolescence. The Spirit does not wait for intellectual maturity. The whole person, every sense, every faculty, is claimed for God from the very beginning.

Eucharist

The newly baptized and chrismated Christian then receives the Eucharist, the Body and Blood of Christ under the forms of bread and wine. This is the climax of initiation and the ongoing center of Christian life. In the Eastern tradition, the Eucharist is received from a spoon that contains both the consecrated bread and wine together, a practice called intinction.

The Eucharist is not an optional devotion. It is the food of immortality, the medicine of the soul, the real presence of Christ entering the communicant's body. To receive the Eucharist is to be united, physically, sacramentally, mystically, with the risen Lord.

Confession: The Return

Confession (also called the Mystery of Repentance) is not a courtroom procedure. In the Eastern practice, penitent and priest stand together before an icon of Christ. The priest is a witness, not a judge. A physician diagnosing the illness, not a magistrate pronouncing sentence. The prayer of absolution asks God to "forgive, remit, and pardon" the sins confessed, and to "reconcile and unite" the penitent to the Church.

The penances assigned, additional prayer, fasting, prostrations, acts of mercy, are therapeutic, not punitive. Exercises designed to strengthen the weakened spiritual muscles that allowed the sin in the first place.

…breaking one and the same bread, which is the medicine of immortality, and the antidote to prevent us from dying, but which causes that we should live forever in Jesus Christ.
— St. Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Ephesians 20 (ANF translation, c. 107 AD)

Marriage: The Little Church

Marriage in the East is called the "crowning," because the bride and groom are literally crowned during the ceremony. The crowns symbolize both the glory of the marital vocation and the martyrdom it entails (the crowns are martyrs' crowns as much as royal ones). Marriage is understood as a path to theosis, the spouses helping each other grow toward God through the daily sacrifices of shared life.

The Eastern marriage service contains no vows.

The couple does not promise each other anything in words. They are crowned by the Church, and the crowning itself is the sacramental act. God joins them; they do not join themselves. The family they create is called the "little church" (ecclesia domestica), a microcosm of the larger Church with its own prayers, its own icons, its own rhythm of fasting and feasting.

Holy Orders: Serving at the Altar

Holy Orders is the Mystery by which men are ordained to the diaconate, the priesthood, or the episcopate. Ordination takes place during the Divine Liturgy, in the presence of the assembled community, because ministry is not a private appointment. It is a public commissioning by the Church.

Married men may be ordained deacons and priests in the Eastern tradition. Bishops are chosen from among celibate clergy. This is not a theological principle but an ancient disciplinary practice, and it has given the Eastern Church a married parish clergy quite different in character from the celibate Latin priesthood.

Holy Unction: Healing of Body and Soul

Holy Unction is the anointing of the sick, and not only the dying. In the Eastern tradition, it is administered not as "Last Rites" (a common misconception) but as a prayer for healing that can be received multiple times throughout life. During Great Lent, many parishes offer a communal service of Holy Unction for all the faithful, recognizing that everyone carries some wound in need of the divine Physician's touch.

The service is beautiful and elaborate: seven Epistle readings, seven Gospel readings, seven anointings with blessed oil. The number seven signifies completeness. The whole person, in every dimension, presented to God for healing.

Mystery, Not Magic

A final word about how the East understands the Mysteries. They are not magical formulas. They do not work automatically, independent of the faith and disposition of the participants. A person who receives the Eucharist without repentance does not receive grace; as St. Paul warned in 1 Corinthians 11, such a person receives judgment. A confession without genuine contrition is empty words.

But neither are the Mysteries merely symbols, beautiful rituals that point to a spiritual reality happening elsewhere. The East insists that the Mysteries are real encounters with God. The bread really becomes the Body. The water really regenerates. The oil really heals. The physical and the spiritual are not separate categories in Eastern thought; they interpenetrate, and the Mysteries are the places where this interpenetration is most visible.

This is why the East calls them Mysteries and not merely sacraments. They cannot be fully explained. They can only be entered into, with faith, with repentance, with awe, and experienced.

The next step moves from what Byzantine Christians believe to how they live, beginning with prayer: the breath of the spiritual life, and the practice that sustains everything else.

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on the Holy Mysteries
▾

Christ Our Pascha §§ 432–529 provides the most comprehensive treatment of the seven mysteries (sacraments). The catechism presents them not as isolated rituals but as encounters with Christ: "In the Holy Mysteries, the Church experiences the saving presence of Christ" (§432). Each mystery addresses a dimension of human life — birth, growth, nourishment, healing, vocation, community — and transforms it by grace.

Practice

The Mysteries are not theology to be learned but grace to be received. If you are Catholic, you are welcome to receive the Eucharist at a Byzantine Catholic parish. If you are not yet Catholic, you are welcome to attend, to pray, to receive antidoron (blessed bread) at the end. Either way: go. Stand in the presence of these mysteries. Let them work on you before you try to work them out.
