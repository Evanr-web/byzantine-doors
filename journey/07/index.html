The Holy Spirit

The Lord and Giver of Life — Pentecost, chrismation, and the Spirit's quiet work in every prayer.

There is a prayer that every Byzantine Christian knows by heart. It is said before meals, before meetings, before any undertaking of any significance. The first prayer taught to children and the last prayer said at night. It begins: "O Heavenly King, Comforter, Spirit of Truth, who art everywhere present and fillest all things…"

This prayer is addressed not to the Father, not to the Son, but to the Holy Spirit.

The fact that Eastern Christians begin nearly everything by invoking the Spirit tells you something essential about how they understand the third Person of the Trinity. In Western Christianity, the Spirit can sometimes feel like the forgotten member, acknowledged in the Creed, invoked at Confirmation, but often overshadowed by the Father and the Son in popular devotion. In the East, the Spirit is everywhere. The breath of the Church, the fire of the sacraments, the quiet presence in every act of prayer. Without the Spirit, Eastern Christians would say, nothing in the Christian life works at all.

Pentecost: The Church's Birthday

Fifty days after Pascha, the Church celebrates Pentecost: the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the Apostles gathered in the upper room. The account in Acts 2 is dramatic. A rush of wind, tongues of fire settling on each head, the sudden ability to speak in languages they had never learned. The crowd outside thinks they are drunk. Peter stands up and explains: this is not wine. This is God.

Eastern Christianity understands Pentecost not merely as a historical event but as the birthday of the Church. Before Pentecost, the Apostles were a group of frightened individuals hiding behind locked doors. After Pentecost, they were the Church, a single Body animated by a single Spirit, pouring into the streets to preach, to heal, to baptize, to die.

The icon is worth studying. The Apostles are seated in a semicircle, not in a hierarchy, but as equals. Above each one, a tongue of fire descends. At the top of the semicircle, the seat is often left empty or contains a book (the Gospel): Christ has ascended, and the Spirit now acts through the Church. At the bottom, in a dark archway, stands a figure sometimes labeled "Cosmos," the world itself, waiting to receive the light that the Spirit brings through the Church's witness.

As sunlight falling on bright and transparent bodies makes them shine brilliantly, so souls that carry the Spirit become spiritual themselves and send forth grace to others.
— St. Basil the Great, On the Holy Spirit, 4th century

The Spirit in the Sacraments

The Holy Spirit is the active agent in every sacrament. This is perhaps the most distinctive feature of Eastern sacramental theology.

In the Divine Liturgy, the most solemn moment is not the recitation of Christ's words, "This is my Body… This is my Blood," though those words are certainly spoken. The decisive moment is the epiklesis: the prayer in which the priest calls down the Holy Spirit upon the bread and wine to transform them into the Body and Blood of Christ. "Send down your Holy Spirit upon us and upon these gifts here set forth," the priest prays, and the congregation responds, "Amen."

This is not a formula that works automatically, like a spell. It is a prayer, a genuine petition to a Person. The transformation of the bread and wine is not something the priest does; it is something the Spirit accomplishes in response to the Church's prayer.

In Baptism, the water is blessed by the invocation of the Spirit before the candidate enters it. The water becomes, in the Church's understanding, a kind of Jordan River: the same Spirit who descended upon Christ at His baptism now descends upon the one being baptized.

In Chrismation, which immediately follows Baptism in the Eastern tradition, the newly baptized is anointed with holy chrism (a specially consecrated oil) on the forehead, eyes, nostrils, lips, ears, chest, hands, and feet, with the words: "The seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit." Every sense, every faculty, claimed for God. This is the Eastern equivalent of the Western sacrament of Confirmation, but it is administered to infants. The Spirit does not wait for the age of reason.

The Spirit in Daily Prayer

The Holy Spirit is not confined to liturgical moments. Eastern spiritual writers describe the Spirit as the hidden partner in every act of prayer. When you pray, you are not alone. The Spirit prays within you, shaping half-formed thoughts into something God can receive.

St. Paul wrote that "the Spirit intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words" (Romans 8:26), and Eastern Christianity takes this experientially. The Jesus Prayer, "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner," is understood to be a cooperation between the human will and the Holy Spirit. The one who prays provides the intention and the persistence; the Spirit provides the power and the depth.

The great spiritual writers of the East, the Desert Fathers, the authors of the Philokalia, the monks of Athos, describe a progression in prayer. At first, prayer is an act of the lips. Then it becomes an act of the mind. Finally, it "descends into the heart," which is to say, the Spirit carries it from the surface of conscious effort into the deep center of the person, where it becomes continuous, effortless, and joyful. This is not mystical abstraction. Countless Eastern Christians have experienced it over two millennia.

The true aim of the Christian life is the acquisition of the Holy Spirit.
— St. Seraphim of Sarov, 19th century

A Brief Word on the Filioque

Eastern and Western Christians disagree about the Holy Spirit. The disagreement centers on a single Latin word: filioque, "and the Son." The original Nicene Creed (381 AD) states that the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." At some point in the early medieval West, the words "and the Son" were added, so that the Spirit "proceeds from the Father and the Son."

For Eastern Christians, this addition is problematic, both because it was made without the consent of an ecumenical council and because it seems to alter the relationship among the Persons of the Trinity. The East maintains that the Father alone is the Source (monarchia) of both the Son and the Spirit, and that the filioque risks subordinating the Spirit to the Son.

This is a genuine theological difference, and it has contributed to centuries of division. But many theologians on both sides, including recent popes and patriarchs, have worked toward a shared understanding. The Eastern position is not that the Son has no role in the Spirit's mission, but that the language of "procession" must be used carefully. A conversation still in progress, better served by patience than by polemic.

St. Basil's On the Holy Spirit is the place to start for anyone wanting the Eastern position stated with precision and force.

The Trisagion and the Life of the Spirit

Three times in the Divine Liturgy, the congregation sings the Trisagion: "Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us." One of the oldest hymns in the Christian tradition, a prayer addressed to the Trinity, three times holy, echoing the song of the seraphim in Isaiah's vision of heaven.

The repetition is deliberate. Eastern worship is not afraid of repetition, because the Spirit works through repetition the way water works through stone. Slowly, patiently, shaping the soul by gradual pressure rather than dramatic force. The Trisagion is said three times. The Lord's Prayer is preceded by silence. The Jesus Prayer is repeated thousands of times. In each case, the human being is creating space, and the Spirit is filling it.

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on the Holy Spirit
▾

Christ Our Pascha §§ 101–125 presents the Holy Spirit as "the Lord and Giver of Life" who proceeds from the Father, is worshipped and glorified together with the Father and the Son, and is active in every aspect of the Church's life. The catechism emphasizes the Spirit's role in the sacraments: it is the Spirit who consecrates the Eucharistic gifts (§114), seals the newly baptized in chrismation (§110), and empowers every Christian for mission and holiness.

Practice

The Holy Spirit is not a concept to be understood but a presence to be experienced. The next time you are in a Byzantine church, watch for the epiklesis — the moment when the priest calls down the Holy Spirit upon the gifts. That is the Spirit at work. In the meantime, begin each morning with the prayer: O Heavenly King, Comforter, Spirit of Truth, who art everywhere present and fillest all things, Treasury of good things and Giver of life: come and dwell in us.
