Icons — Windows into Heaven

Visual theology — why the walls are covered in paintings, and what they're actually saying.

Home › Journey › Icons — Windows into Heaven

Read time 7 min read

Walk into any Byzantine church and the first thing that confronts you is not a sermon, not a creed, not a book. It is an image. Images everywhere. Painted on walls, mounted on stands, covering the iconostasis from floor to ceiling, tucked into corners where candles burn low. Faces, mostly. Calm, serious faces with enormous eyes, looking directly at you. Not past you, not above you. At you.

These are icons. They are not what you think they are.

If you come from a Western background, your first instinct might be to evaluate them as art. Are they beautiful? Technically skilled? Some are. Some are rough, almost primitive-looking. But evaluating an icon on its artistic merit is like evaluating a window on the quality of its glass. You're missing the point. The point is what you see through it.

Theology in Color

In the Byzantine tradition, an icon is not art. It is theology written in color. Every element carries theological meaning: the position of the hands, the tilt of the head, the colors of the garments, the arrangement of figures. An iconographer does not paint from imagination. The iconographer paints from tradition, following rules (called a "canon") handed down for centuries. The icon is not self-expression. It is transmission.

Consider the most famous icon ever painted: Andrei Rublev's Trinity, created in Russia around 1410. Three figures sit around a table, their bodies forming a circle, their gazes directed toward one another and toward the viewer. At first glance, it depicts three angels who visited Abraham at the Oak of Mamre (Genesis 18). But every Orthodox and Byzantine Catholic Christian who looks at this icon sees the Holy Trinity: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, in perfect communion, inviting the viewer into their circle.

Notice the open space at the front of the table. There is room for one more. That space, the tradition says, is for you.

The Oldest Surviving Icon

Travel to the Monastery of Saint Catherine at the foot of Mount Sinai, one of the oldest continuously operating monasteries in the world, and you will find an icon that has survived since the sixth century. A portrait of Christ, painted in encaustic (hot wax and pigment), startling in its directness.

Look at the face. Art historians have noted that it is deliberately asymmetrical. The right side (Christ's left) is calm, serene, compassionate. The left side is sterner, more intense, almost judicial. Two natures in one face: mercy and justice, human tenderness and divine authority. This is not an accident. This is Christology rendered in paint.

The icon predates the great iconoclastic controversies by two centuries, yet it already embodies the theological principles that would later be formally defended: that depicting Christ is not merely permitted but necessary, because God chose to become visible in human flesh.

The Crisis: Iconoclasm

For over a century (roughly 726–843 AD), the Byzantine Empire was torn apart by the question of images. Emperors ordered icons destroyed. Monks who defended them were tortured and killed. The word iconoclasm, "image-breaking," comes from this period.

The iconoclasts argued that depicting Christ was either impossible (because his divine nature cannot be depicted) or idolatrous (because it reduces God to mere matter). The defenders of icons, the iconodules, argued something more radical: that because God became human in Jesus Christ, matter itself has been sanctified. To refuse to depict Christ is to deny the Incarnation. If God truly took on flesh, then flesh and wood and paint and gold can bear the presence of the divine.

I do not worship matter. I worship the God of matter, who became matter for my sake, who willed to take His abode in matter, who worked out my salvation through matter.
— St. John of Damascus, On the Divine Images, First Apology (Louth translation)

The Seventh Ecumenical Council (787 AD, at Nicaea) resolved the question definitively: icons are to be venerated, not worshipped, but venerated. The distinction is crucial. Worship (latreia) belongs to God alone. Veneration (proskynesis), honor, reverence, a kiss, passes through the image to the person depicted. When you kiss an icon of Christ, you are not worshipping wood and paint. You are greeting the Person who the icon makes present.

St. John of Damascus's On the Divine Images remains the definitive defense. It is short, fierce, and still relevant to every argument about religious art.

Icon vs. Idol

This is perhaps the most common misunderstanding outsiders bring to a Byzantine church. "Isn't this idol worship?"

The answer, from the tradition's own perspective, is precisely the opposite. An idol claims to be a god. An icon points to a person. An idol is a dead end: the wood or stone is all there is. An icon is a window. You look through it to the living reality beyond. The gold background on most icons is not mere decoration. It represents the uncreated light of God, a light that has no source in this world, that shines from beyond.

Think of a photograph of someone you love. You might carry it in your wallet, prop it on your desk, even kiss it when they're far away. No one accuses you of worshipping paper and ink. You know the photo is not the person. But the photo makes the person present to you in a way that nothing else quite can. Icons function similarly, but in a theological register, connecting the visible to the invisible.

The Mother of God

No figure appears more often in Byzantine iconography than the Theotokos, the Mother of God. Her image is always on the left side of the iconostasis, often holding the Christ child. One of the most revered icons in the world is the Theotokos of Vladimir, painted in Constantinople in the twelfth century and brought to Russia, where it has been venerated for nearly a millennium.

Look at her eyes. Warmth, but also a deep sadness. A knowledge of the suffering to come. The Christ child presses his cheek to hers, his small hand reaching around her neck. This style is called Eleousa, "tenderness." It is, perhaps, the most human image in all of Christian art.

And yet the theological statement is profound: this child, nestled against his mother's cheek, is the God who created the universe. The infinite has become small enough to be held.

Why This Matters

Icons matter because they embody a conviction at the heart of Eastern Christianity: that the material world is not opposed to the spiritual world. Matter can be holy. Bodies can be transfigured. The physical and the spiritual are not enemies. They are meant to be united, just as divinity and humanity are united in Christ.

This is why the resolution of the iconoclastic controversy is celebrated every year on the first Sunday of Lent, the Sunday of Orthodoxy, with a procession of icons around the church. It is not a celebration of art. It is a celebration of the Incarnation itself: that God entered the material world and, in doing so, made it possible for the material world to bear the image of God.

When you stand before an icon, you are not looking at a relic of the past. You are standing at a window. And on the other side, someone is looking back.
