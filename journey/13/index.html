Step 13

The Saints

A cloud of witnesses — the men, women, and fools-for-Christ who show us what holiness looks like.

The Eastern Church is crowded. Walk into any Byzantine church and you are immediately surrounded, not by the living congregation alone, but by the saints. They line the walls in frescoes and mosaics. They fill the iconostasis in painted panels. They gaze from every surface with those large, luminous eyes that seem to see something you cannot.

A visitor may find this overwhelming. An Eastern Christian finds it comforting.

The saints are not decorations or historical figures. They are present. They are family. They are proof that the Christian life actually works. When the Epistle to the Hebrews speaks of "so great a cloud of witnesses" surrounding the faithful, the East takes this literally. The saints have run the race and finished it. They have been transfigured by the grace of God, not made perfect in some sterile, inhuman way, but genuinely transformed: their passions ordered, their hearts purified, their bodies sometimes even giving off a fragrance of holiness after death.

They are what humanity was created to be.

How Saints Are Recognized

The Eastern process of recognizing saints differs sharply from the Roman Catholic system of formal canonization, with its postulators, devil's advocates, and required miracles verified by medical tribunals. In the East, recognition of sanctity is more organic. It rises from the grassroots.

A person's holiness is recognized first by the local community who knew them. Their relics may be found to be incorrupt (a sign, though not a requirement, of sanctity). Miracles are reported at their tomb or through their intercession. Hymns are composed in their honor. Eventually, the local bishop, sometimes a synod, formally acknowledges what the faithful already know: this person is a saint. The process can take years or centuries. There is no rush. The saints are patient.

This organic approach means that the Eastern calendar of saints is vast and varied, and occasionally surprising. It includes emperors and slaves, scholars and illiterates, warriors and pacifists, married couples and hermits. Holiness, in the Eastern view, is not confined to any single form of life. It is the transformation of any life by grace.

God became human so that humans might become God.
— St. Athanasius of Alexandria, On the Incarnation 54.3, 4th century

This is the whole purpose of the Christian life — and the saints are living proof that it is possible.

Types of Saints

The Eastern Church recognizes several categories, each representing a different way of following Christ:

Martyrs — those who died for the faith. The Church's foundation stones. Their feasts are among the oldest in the calendar.

Confessors — those who suffered for the faith but survived. Many endured torture, exile, or imprisonment under Byzantine emperors or Communist persecution without renouncing Christ.

Monastics — monks and nuns whose holiness was forged in the crucible of the ascetic life. The Egyptian desert fathers, the monks of Mount Athos, the startsy of Russia. Among the most beloved saints of the East. The Sayings of the Desert Fathers is the best entry point: short, sharp, and startlingly contemporary.

Holy Fools (yurodivy) — perhaps the most distinctively Eastern category. Saints who deliberately adopted the appearance of madness for Christ's sake, living as vagrants, saying outrageous things, using their apparent lunacy to speak truth to power. St. Basil of Moscow, after whom the famous cathedral is named, walked naked through the streets and once threw rocks at the houses of the pious while kissing the walls of taverns. Because, he said, demons were fleeing the taverns while angels wept at the houses.

Right-Believing Rulers — kings, queens, and emperors who used their power justly and in service of the faith. St. Vladimir of Kyiv, who baptized the Rus', and St. Helena, who discovered the True Cross, are among the most prominent.

The Theotokos — First Among the Saints

No discussion of the saints can proceed without the one who stands first among them: the Theotokos, the Mother of God. Eastern Christianity's devotion to Mary is intense, pervasive, and theologically grounded. She is not merely a good example or a historical figure. She is the one who made the Incarnation possible by her free "yes" to the angel Gabriel, and in that "yes," she reversed the "no" of Eve and opened the door to salvation.

The title "Theotokos," literally "God-bearer," was solemnly affirmed at the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD. It is not primarily a statement about Mary; it is a statement about Christ. If the child she bore was truly God incarnate, then she is truly the Mother of God. To deny the title is to deny the Incarnation itself.

In Eastern churches, the Theotokos occupies the place of honor in the apse, the curved wall behind the altar, often depicted with arms raised in prayer, with the Christ Child before her. She is invoked in virtually every service. The troparion and kontakion of her feasts are among the most beautiful hymns in the Eastern repertoire.

In you, O Full of Grace, all creation rejoices — the assembly of angels and the race of men. O sanctified temple and spiritual paradise, the glory of virgins!
— 'In Thee Rejoiceth,' hymn sung at the Liturgy of St. Basil the Great

Icons, Feast Days, and Patronal Saints

The saints are not abstract presences. They are encountered concretely, in their icons, on their feast days, and through the patronal relationship. Every Eastern Christian receives the name of a saint at baptism and celebrates that saint's feast day as their name day. In many Eastern cultures, this is a celebration more important than a birthday. The saint whose name you bear is your heavenly patron, your intercessor, your model.

Each parish, too, has a patronal saint, the saint to whom the church is dedicated. The parish celebrates this saint's feast with special solemnity, often with a festal Liturgy followed by a communal meal. The connection is personal and local: St. Nicholas is not merely a figure in a book but the patron of this church, these people, this community.

A Living Relationship

Perhaps the most important thing to understand about the Eastern approach to the saints is that it is not historical. The saints are not in the past. They are more alive now than they were on earth, because death, for those who have been united to the Risen Christ, is not an ending but a transition. The Church prays with the saints, not merely about them.

This is why an Eastern Christian will ask St. Nicholas to pray for them with the same naturalness as asking a living friend. It is why relics, the physical remains of the saints, are venerated with such reverence: the body that was a temple of the Holy Spirit in life does not cease to be holy in death. It is why the saints' icons are kissed, their troparion sung, their stories told and retold.

The saints are not a monument to past achievement. They are ongoing proof that grace works, that theosis is real, that the God who transformed a tax collector into an evangelist and a persecutor into an apostle is still in the business of making saints. Today, now, out of the most unlikely material.

Including, the Church dares to hope, out of you.

The beliefs, practices, and lived spirituality of Eastern Christianity have now been explored. The next step turns to a harder question: why is the Christian East divided from the Christian West, and what hope is there for reunion?

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on the Saints
▾

Christ Our Pascha §§ 163–217 discusses the veneration of saints, the Theotokos, and the communion of saints. "The saints are not simply moral examples; they are living witnesses to the reality of theosis" (§168). The catechism affirms that the saints intercede for the living, that icons make their presence accessible, and that the entire Church — both in heaven and on earth — constitutes one worshipping community.

Practice

Choose one saint. Just one. Read their life. Pray to them for a week. Put their icon somewhere you will see it daily. The saints are not historical curiosities. They are alive, they are praying for you, and they want to help. A good place to start: the saint whose name you share, or the saint commemorated on your birthday.
