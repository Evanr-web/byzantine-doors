Step 03

The Holy Trinity

Three Persons, One God — the mystery at the heart of everything.

Home › Journey › The Holy Trinity
Step 03 of 16
Read time 7 min read

Before it is a doctrine, the Trinity is an experience. Walk into a Byzantine liturgy and within the first minutes you will hear it. The deacon calls out: "In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit." The choir responds: "Amen." Before a single theological argument is made, before a single creed is recited, the Trinity is there. Not as a problem to be solved, but as the ocean you are swimming in.

Most Christians who say they believe in the Trinity have no idea what it actually means. It has become a formula, "three in one," repeated without comprehension, like a password. Some imagine God as a single person wearing three different hats. Others imagine a committee of three separate gods who happen to agree on everything.

Both images are wrong. Deeply, importantly wrong.

The Eastern tradition insists that the Trinity is not a puzzle at the margins of the faith. It is the center. Everything flows from it: creation, salvation, prayer, the meaning of human existence. Get the Trinity wrong, and everything else eventually distorts.

Where You Hear It

In the Byzantine liturgy, Trinitarian language saturates everything. The Trisagion, one of the most ancient hymns in Christianity, is sung at every Divine Liturgy:

Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us.

Repeated three times. Three times for three Persons. This hymn has been sung in Christian worship since at least the fifth century. Tradition holds that it was revealed by angels.

Then there is the doxology that punctuates prayers throughout the day: "Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages." Byzantine Christians say this dozens of times a day if they follow the daily prayer cycle. It is not decoration. It is orientation: a constant turning of the mind and heart toward the reality of who God is.

And at every baptism, the formula given by Christ himself: "I baptize you in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit." Note: not "names," plural. Name, singular. One God. Three Persons. The grammar itself is theology.

The Cappadocian Breakthrough

In the fourth century, three theologians from Cappadocia (modern-day central Turkey), Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa, gave the Church the conceptual language it needed to articulate what it had always believed.

Their key insight was the distinction between ousia (essence, or "what God is") and hypostasis (person, or "who God is"). God has one essence: one divine nature, one divine will, one divine energy. But this one essence exists in three distinct Persons, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit.

The Persons are not parts of God. Each Person is fully God. Not one-third of God, not a different "mode" of God, but the whole of God. And yet they are not three Gods. They are distinguished not by what they are (they share the same essence) but by their relations to one another: the Father begets the Son, the Son is begotten of the Father, and the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father.

If your head is spinning, that's appropriate.

No sooner do I conceive of the One than I am illumined by the splendor of the Three; no sooner do I distinguish them than I am carried back to the One.
— St. Gregory the Theologian, 4th century

Gregory was not embarrassed by the difficulty. He considered it evidence that the doctrine was true, because a God simple enough to be fully grasped by the human mind would not be God. St. Basil's treatise On the Holy Spirit remains one of the finest sustained arguments in all of Christian theology. It repays careful reading.

Communion, Not Isolation

Here is the part that changes everything.

If God is Trinity, if the deepest reality in the universe is not a solitary monarch but a communion of Persons in perfect love, then love is not something God does. Love is what God is. Not as a sentiment or an emotion, but as the fundamental structure of ultimate reality.

A solitary God could be powerful. A solitary God could be wise. But a solitary God could not, in himself, be love, because love requires an other. The Trinity means that otherness, relationship, and self-giving existed before the universe began. God did not create the world because he was lonely. He created it as an overflow of the love that already existed within himself.

This is why Rublev's icon of the Trinity has become the single most important image in Eastern Christianity. Look at it again:

Three figures, inclined toward one another, their bodies forming a circle. Their gazes are gentle, attentive, receptive. There is no hierarchy of posture. No one is dominating, no one is diminished. The cup on the table between them is the Eucharist, the self-offering of love. And the open space at the front of the table invites the viewer to take a seat.

Not a mathematical puzzle. An eternal exchange of love so abundant that it spills over into creation, into history, into your life, right now.

Why It Matters for You

In the Eastern tradition, the purpose of human existence is theosis, becoming united with God by grace, participating in the divine life without losing your own identity. And the divine life is Trinitarian. To be drawn into God is to be drawn into communion. It is to learn the pattern of self-giving love that the three Persons have practiced from all eternity.

This has immediate, practical consequences.

You were made for relationship. If God himself is not solitary, then the deepest truth about human beings, made in God's image, is that they are not meant to be alone. Isolation is not holiness. Community is not a concession to weakness.

Love is not about absorption. In the Trinity, the Persons do not merge into one. The Father does not cease to be the Father; the Son does not dissolve into the Spirit. Unity and distinction coexist. Real love, divine love, does not erase the other. It honors the other precisely as other, while remaining in perfect unity.

Power is not what you think it is. The Father is called the "source" of the Trinity, not because he is more powerful, but because he eternally gives himself away. He begets the Son; he breathes forth the Spirit. In the Trinity, the origin of all things is not grasping but giving.

The Trinity is not a doctrine you have to believe before you can enter the Church. It is the reality you discover, gradually, as you go deeper. Every prayer, every sacrament, every act of love between human beings is, whether anyone knows it or not, a participation in the Trinitarian life of God.

The door is open. There is a seat at the table.

For a deeper exploration, Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church devotes its finest chapters to the Trinity. It is dense but rewarding.

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on the Holy Trinity
▾

The catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church treats the Trinity in §§ 42–70, beginning with the revelation of the triune God in Scripture and the Church's gradual articulation of this mystery through the ecumenical councils. "God is not a solitary being but a communion of Persons" (§43). The catechism emphasizes that Trinitarian theology is not abstract speculation but the foundation of Christian prayer: every liturgical action begins and ends with the invocation of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Practice

This week, pay attention to how often the Trinity appears in the liturgy. The Sign of the Cross. The opening blessing. The Trisagion. The Creed. The Great Doxology. The dismissal. Begin and end each day by making the Sign of the Cross slowly, deliberately: In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit.
