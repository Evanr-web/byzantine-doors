Step 16

Coming Home

What it looks like to walk through the doors — and what you might find on the other side.

Sixteen steps. From that first glimpse through heavy wooden doors, through Trinitarian theology and icons, through sacraments and saints and the long painful history of division. Maybe you started out of idle curiosity. Maybe something drew you forward that you cannot quite name. Whatever brought you here, the question is simple now.

What next?

This is not a sales pitch. Eastern Christianity does not do sales pitches. It has a Liturgy, an open door, and an ancient conviction that beauty and truth, encountered honestly, do their own work.

For the Curious

Just come.

Attend a Divine Liturgy. No call ahead. No appointment. No prerequisites. The service will be longer than you expect, probably 60 to 90 minutes. Almost everything is sung. There will be incense. The priest will face the altar for much of it, not the congregation. People will cross themselves and bow and prostrate at moments you cannot predict.

This is fine. No one expects you to know the choreography. Stand when others stand. Sit when others sit. Let it wash over you.

You will not be asked to fill out a visitor card. You will not be cornered by a greeter with a name tag. But if it is a healthy parish, someone will invite you to coffee hour afterward and hand you a plate of food before you have finished introducing yourself. Accept the food. Ask the questions.

We knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth, for on earth there is no such splendor or beauty. We only know that God dwells there among men.
— The emissaries of Prince Vladimir of Kyiv, upon attending the Divine Liturgy at Hagia Sophia, 987 AD

For the Serious Inquirer

If curiosity has grown into something that will not let go, talk to the priest. Eastern priests are generally accessible. They are accustomed to inquirers. Tell them where you are: what drew you, what questions remain, what holds you back.

Most Byzantine Catholic parishes have a catechetical process for those preparing to enter the Church. Not a class you pass or fail. A period of learning, prayer, and discernment, typically several months to a year. You learn the faith, the sacraments, the liturgical year, the spiritual life. You begin attending Liturgy regularly, fasting, developing a prayer rule.

The culmination is reception through the sacraments of initiation: Baptism (if unbaptized), Chrismation, and the Eucharist, all together. If you have been baptized in another Christian tradition, your baptism is typically recognized. You are received through Chrismation and Communion. Kallistos Ware's The Orthodox Way is an excellent companion for this period of discernment.

For Latin Catholics

You have a standing invitation.

Attend any Byzantine Catholic parish. Participate fully. Receive the Eucharist. No permission needed. The Byzantine Catholic churches are in full communion with Rome. What is celebrated at that altar is the same Body and Blood you receive at your Latin parish, offered in a different rite within the same Catholic communion.

Many Latin Catholics discover in the Byzantine tradition something they had been missing without knowing it. A deeper sense of mystery. A richer theology of the spiritual life. Elements of early Christianity that the West has attenuated. You can attend both. Some eventually transfer their canonical enrollment to a Byzantine Catholic church, a process requiring both bishops' permission but neither unusual nor difficult.

You lose nothing by exploring. You gain a second lung.

For Orthodox Christians

This is the most delicate section in the entire journey. The relationship between Byzantine Catholics and Orthodox is complex, painful, and full of history. No pretending otherwise.

Byzantine Catholics and Orthodox share virtually everything: the same Liturgy, the same theology, the same spirituality, the same Fathers, the same icons. The difference is ecclesiological. The question of communion with Rome. For some Orthodox, Byzantine Catholics represent an unacceptable compromise. For Byzantine Catholics, the opposite: a bridge, a witness that Eastern and Western Christianity can coexist in communion.

If you are Orthodox and feel drawn toward communion with Rome, the door is open. A Byzantine Catholic parish will feel like home. The transition involves no repudiation of your Orthodox faith or practice. Same Liturgy. Same icons. Same spiritual traditions. What changes is canonical reality.

No proselytizing. No claims of monopoly. Simply existence, and the rest left to the Holy Spirit.

Love your own rite, your own Church, your own tradition — but never at the expense of love for your brothers and sisters in Christ, wherever they may worship.
— Metropolitan Andrey Sheptytsky

For Those New to Christianity

Perhaps you arrived here with no Christian background at all. The Byzantine tradition just happened to be the form in which you first encountered it.

Welcome.

The Eastern Church does not ask you to check your intellect at the door or accept propositions on blind faith. It asks you to enter an experience: worship, beauty, prayer, community. To see whether, within that experience, you encounter the living God. The theology comes later, as reflection on the encounter. The encounter comes first.

Talk to a priest. Attend Liturgy. Read the Gospels. Pray, even if uncertain anyone is listening. The Eastern tradition has great patience with seekers. Some saints took decades to find their way. There is no deadline.

Finding a Parish

United States: The Byzantine Catholic Metropolitan Church of Pittsburgh maintains a parish directory. The Ruthenian, Ukrainian, Melkite, and other Eastern Catholic eparchies each have locators on their websites.

Canada: The Ukrainian Catholic Eparchy of Edmonton, the Eparchy of Toronto, and the Slovak Eparchy of Saints Cyril and Methodius all maintain directories.

Worldwide: The Catholic Near East Welfare Association (CNEWA) provides information on Eastern Catholic communities globally.

If no Byzantine Catholic parish exists near you, an Orthodox parish will offer a virtually identical liturgical experience, though intercommunion is not possible. Any Latin Catholic parish will welcome you into the same communion, even if the rite differs.

Not a Conclusion

A website can only do so much. The real journey begins when you walk through actual doors into an actual church, where actual people are singing actual hymns to a God they believe is actually present.

The Theotokos waits in the apse. The saints watch from the walls. The incense rises. The priest chants: "Blessed is the Kingdom of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages."

"Amen."

Come and see.
