Step 01

What Happens Behind Those Doors?

The Divine Liturgy as first encounter — walking into a Byzantine church for the very first time.

Home › Journey › What Happens Behind Those Doors?
Step 01 of 16
Read time 6 min read

Divine LiturgyIconostasisTheotokos

You push through the heavy wooden doors and step inside. The world you just left falls away: traffic, notifications, the relentless scroll of daily life. It takes a moment for your eyes to adjust. The space is dimmer than expected, lit not by fluorescent panels but by the warm flicker of beeswax candles and the distant glow of oil lamps hanging from chains. The air is different here, thick with something sweet and ancient. Frankincense, rising in slow blue-grey curls from a censer somewhere ahead.

This is a Byzantine church. Nothing about it is accidental.

The walls hit you first. They are not bare. They are alive, covered on almost every surface with paintings. Saints look out from every direction, their eyes calm, direct, unblinking. Above, filling the dome, a figure gazes down with an expression that is somehow both fierce and tender. This is Christ, not as a teacher or a friend, but as the Pantocrator, the ruler of all things. His right hand is raised in blessing. In his left, he holds a book.

You are being looked at as much as you are looking.

A Wall of Gold and Mystery

Ahead stands a wall of icons, the iconostasis, stretching from floor to ceiling. A shimmering barrier between where you stand and something hidden beyond. In its center are three doors. The largest pair, the Royal Doors, are closed. Through them, you catch glimpses of movement: a priest in gold vestments, the corner of an altar draped in white cloth, the flicker of candles in a space you cannot enter.

This is not a stage. There is no audience seating, no projection screen, no worship band warming up.

The architecture itself is making a statement: some things are holy. Some things are hidden. You are being invited in, but there is always more beyond what you can see.

Consider what it is you are about to approach; consider that we, who are on earth, have been judged worthy of things in heaven. We are standing alongside the Cherubim. We take part in the hymns of the Seraphim.
— St. John Chrysostom, Homily 3 on Colossians (NPNF translation)

The Liturgy Begins

Then the singing starts. Not from a stage. From everywhere. The voices might come from a choir hidden in a loft, or from the congregation itself, or from a cantor standing at a simple wooden stand. There are no instruments. Just human voices, weaving harmonies that seem older than the building itself.

The service about to unfold is called the Divine Liturgy. It is not a lecture, not a concert, not a motivational seminar. According to the people who worship here, it is an actual participation in the worship of heaven. The hymns being sung this particular Sunday morning are the same hymns sung in Byzantine churches in Constantinople in the fifth century, in Damascus in the seventh, in Kyiv in the eleventh. The words have survived emperors and invasions, plagues and persecutions.

If that claim sounds audacious, it's meant to. This tradition does not present itself as innovative.

What You See, What You Hear, What You Smell

Throughout the Liturgy, three senses are engaged simultaneously. This is by design.

Sight: The icons surrounding you are not decoration. Each one is placed deliberately: Christ on the right of the Royal Doors, the Theotokos (the Mother of God) on the left, the saint to whom the church is dedicated beside them. The faithful kiss these icons as they enter, touching their foreheads to the painted wood. Light moves constantly, candle flames bending in drafts, the gleam of gold leaf catching the eye.

Sound: The Liturgy is almost entirely sung. Prayers, Scripture readings, responses, all chanted in melodies that range from simple to hauntingly complex. There are moments of near-silence, when the priest prays behind the closed doors and only a murmured hush fills the church. Then the doors open, and the singing swells again.

Smell: Incense is used lavishly. The deacon swings the censer through the church, censing the icons, the altar, the people themselves. In Byzantine theology, incense is not ambiance. It is prayer made visible, rising to God as the Psalms describe: "Let my prayer rise like incense before You."

The Shape of the Liturgy

Without knowing the details, you can sense that the Liturgy has a shape, a dramatic arc. It moves through preparation, through readings and teaching, and builds toward a climax. That climax is the Eucharist: the moment when, according to this tradition, bread and wine become the actual Body and Blood of Christ.

This is not symbolic for them. Not a memorial meal.

It is, they believe, a genuine encounter with the risen Christ, as real as anything that happens outside these walls. More real, in fact. When the Royal Doors open for Communion, the priest emerges carrying a golden chalice and calls out: "With fear of God, with faith and love, draw near." People approach, young and old, men and women, and receive the Eucharist on a small golden spoon. Children are brought forward, sometimes squirming in their parents' arms. There is no age requirement. If you are baptized in this tradition, you receive.

The liturgy is not a "service" we attend, but rather the very being of the Church itself made visible.
— Alexander Schmemann, For the Life of the World

Standing, Crossing, Bowing

One more thing strikes you: the congregation stands. Most of the Liturgy is spent on your feet. People cross themselves, but differently than you might expect. The hand moves from right to left, not left to right, with three fingers pressed together (for the Trinity) and two folded down (for Christ's two natures, human and divine).

Even the way they move their hand is a theological statement.

They bow. They prostrate, forehead to the ground, at certain moments. They venerate icons with kisses. The body is not a passive container sitting in a pew; it is a participant. Worship here involves the whole person: mind, voice, and flesh.

You Don't Have to Understand It All

Here is what no one tells the visitor, but what every person in that church already knows: you are not expected to understand all of this the first time. Or the tenth. The Liturgy is not designed to be fully grasped in a single encounter. It is designed to be inhabited, lived in, Sunday after Sunday, season after season, until the prayers seep into your bones and the rhythms of the liturgical year become the rhythms of your life.

The Byzantine tradition has a word for this kind of knowing. Not intellectual mastery. Experience: a slow, patient, embodied encounter with something that reveals itself gradually, the way dawn reveals a landscape.

You don't need to believe anything to stand in this space. You don't need to cross yourself or bow or kiss an icon. But if you stay, if you let the incense settle around you and the chanting wash over you, you might begin to sense what these people have sensed for two thousand years: that this strange, beautiful, ancient ritual is not a performance about God.

It is, they say, God Himself, present in their midst.

If you read one book about the Liturgy, make it Schmemann's For the Life of the World. It is short, luminous, and unlike anything else ever written about Christian worship.
