Step 15

Two Lungs, One Body

Eastern and Western Christianity as complementary — different emphases within one faith.

In 1995, Pope John Paul II offered an image that stuck. The Church, he wrote, breathes with two lungs: one Eastern, one Western. Neither is sufficient alone. It was not diplomacy. It was a theological confession that the fullness of Christianity cannot be found in either tradition by itself.

That claim cuts in both directions.

It means the West has something the East needs, and the East has something the West has partially forgotten. It also means the current division, however explicable historically, is impoverishing everyone. Both lungs exist. They are not breathing together. The result is a Christianity that gasps where it should sing.

What the West Offers

Eastern Christians sometimes speak as though their tradition is complete in itself. Understandable pride, but not quite honest.

Systematic theology. Thomas Aquinas's Summa Theologiae is a cathedral of the mind. Every question anticipated, every objection met, every doctrine located within a coherent whole. The East, with its preference for apophatic theology and its suspicion of over-systematization, has sometimes lacked this intellectual architecture. There is a difference between holy mystery and intellectual laziness.

Social teaching. From Rerum Novarum in 1891 to Laudato Si' in 2015, Rome has produced a body of social teaching on labor, economics, human rights, war, and the common good that has no real Eastern equivalent. The East has profound things to say about the human person. It has not always said them in forms the modern world can hear.

Juridical clarity. The West's canon law, its structures of authority, its decision-making processes provide a stability the East sometimes lacks. Autocephaly preserves local autonomy. It has also produced jurisdictional confusion, ethnic nationalism, and occasional paralysis.

The Church must breathe with her two lungs! In the first millennium of the history of Christianity, this was expressed by the joint progress of the Churches of the East and the West.
— Pope John Paul II, Ut Unum Sint, 1995

What the East Preserves

The traffic runs both ways. Since the Second Vatican Council, the West has been on a journey of rediscovery. Much of what it is finding has been in the East all along.

Apophatic theology. The West's tradition is predominantly kataphatic: God is good, God is just, God is omnipotent. The East balances this with insistence that God ultimately transcends every human category. God is not merely "very powerful." He is beyond power. This guards against reducing God to a philosophical concept. The West is starting to see the value. Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church is the best introduction to this tradition in English.

Theosis. Salvation as divinization. The West has framed it juridically: sin as crime, grace as pardon, justification as legal declaration. The East frames it therapeutically: sin as sickness, grace as healing, salvation as becoming what God created you to be. Neither is wrong. But the Eastern framework is arguably closer to the early Fathers.

Liturgical richness. The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom has been celebrated in essentially the same form for over 1,500 years. Every sense engaged. Theological content staggering. The West's post-Vatican II simplifications, whatever their intent, left many Latin Catholics sensing a loss. The Eastern liturgy shows what worship looks like when beauty is treated as necessity, not luxury. For a profound exploration of this, read Alexander Schmemann's For the Life of the World.

Synodality. Pope Francis has made this word central to his pontificate. Its roots are deeply Eastern. The East has always governed by consensus, council, and communion rather than decree. The West is beginning to see the wisdom.

Twenty-Three Churches, One Communion

The Catholic Church is not a monolith.

It is a communion of twenty-three sui iuris churches: one Latin, twenty-two Eastern. Each has its own liturgy, its own canon law, its own spiritual patrimony, its own hierarchy. They are not branches of the Latin Church. They are sister churches, equal in dignity.

Ukrainian Greek Catholic. Melkite. Maronite. Syro-Malabar. Romanian Greek Catholic. Ruthenian. And more. Together they represent every major Eastern liturgical tradition: Byzantine, Alexandrian, Antiochene, Armenian, Chaldean.

Within a single Catholic communion, you can find the Roman Mass and the Divine Liturgy. Gregorian chant and Byzantine chant. The rosary and the Jesus Prayer. Thomas Aquinas and Gregory Palamas. This is not a problem to be solved. It is a richness to be celebrated. Kallistos Ware's The Orthodox Church provides excellent context on the Eastern traditions, even from an Orthodox perspective.

The Catholic Church holds in high esteem the institutions, liturgical rites, ecclesiastical traditions, and the established standards of the Christian life of the Eastern Churches.
— Second Vatican Council, Orientalium Ecclesiarum, 1964

Not Better. Not Worse.

The temptation on both sides is to claim superiority. Eastern Christians see legalism and liturgical impoverishment in the West. Western Christians see ethnic particularism and institutional dysfunction in the East. Both caricatures contain grains of truth. Both are profoundly unfair.

The healthier posture is gratitude. The West gave the world the university, the hospital, the systematic defense of human rights, and a missionary energy that carried the Gospel to every continent. The East preserved the liturgy in its ancient fullness, developed a theology of the human person that anticipates modern psychology by a millennium, and produced a monastic tradition of breathtaking depth.

Two lungs. One body. The Body of Christ needs both to breathe fully. And perhaps the greatest gift Byzantine Catholics offer the universal Church is the daily, lived experience of holding East and West together. Not in theory. In worship, in prayer, and in the communion of saints.
