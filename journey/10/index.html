Prayer — Learning to Breathe

The Eastern approach to prayer life — from the Jesus Prayer to the rhythms of the Horologion.

Home › Journey › Prayer — Learning to Breathe

If the sacraments are the lungs of the Church, prayer is breathing itself. Eastern Christianity does not treat prayer as one activity among many, a line item on the spiritual to-do list. Prayer is the atmosphere of the Christian life. Without it, the sacraments become rituals, the icons become decorations, and theology becomes an academic exercise. With it, everything else comes alive.

The Fathers of the East had a phrase for this: pray without ceasing. They meant it literally.

That sounds impossible. Jobs, families, grocery lists. But the Eastern tradition has, over two millennia, developed a remarkably practical approach to unceasing prayer, one that begins not with superhuman effort but with a single sentence, repeated until it sinks from the mind into the heart.

The Jesus Prayer

At the center of Eastern prayer life stands five words that have shaped more saints than any theological treatise: "Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me." Sometimes expanded to "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." This is the Jesus Prayer, and it is the foundation stone of Eastern spirituality.

Deceptively simple. It contains the whole of Christian theology in a single breath: the lordship of Christ, His divinity, the reality of human need, and the appeal to mercy that is the only appropriate response of a creature before its Creator. To pray it once is to say something true. To pray it a thousand times is to begin to live inside that truth.

By the grace of God I am a Christian man, by my actions a great sinner, and by calling a homeless wanderer of the humblest birth who roams from place to place. My worldly goods are a knapsack with some dried bread in it on my back, and in my breast-pocket a Bible. And that is all.
— The Way of a Pilgrim, R.M. French translation

The Jesus Prayer is traditionally accompanied by a prayer rope (chotki or komboskini), a knotted cord, usually of wool, with 33, 50, or 100 knots. The rope serves the same purpose as the Western rosary: it gives the hands something to do while the mind focuses on God. But unlike the rosary, which moves through a cycle of different prayers and mysteries, the prayer rope carries one prayer, repeated. The repetition is the point.

As the anonymous author of The Way of a Pilgrim discovered, the prayer eventually begins to pray itself, running like a quiet stream beneath the surface of daily life. That book, available in many translations, remains the single best introduction to this practice. It reads like a novel and teaches like a spiritual master.

The Rule of Prayer

Every Eastern Christian is encouraged to maintain a rule of prayer, a fixed daily structure of morning and evening prayers. This is not optional devotion for the especially pious; it is the basic framework of the spiritual life, as normal as eating breakfast.

The content varies. Some use the prayers found in prayer books like the Jordanville Prayer Book or the Old Orthodox Prayer Book. These typically include psalms, troparia (short hymns), the Creed, prayers to the Theotokos, and prayers for the living and the dead. Others follow a simplified version: a few psalms, the Jesus Prayer on the rope, and a period of silence.

What matters is consistency, not length. A spiritual father will typically assign a rule calibrated to the person's state of life: a busy mother of small children gets a different rule than a retired monk. The principle mirrors physical exercise. Regular moderate effort produces more growth than sporadic bursts of intensity. Five minutes every morning, faithfully kept, outweighs an hour once a month.

The Psalms

The Psalms are the backbone of Eastern prayer. The entire Psalter, all 150 psalms, is read through every week in monastic communities, and every two weeks in parish practice during Great Lent. The psalms are not merely read; they are prayed. They provide the words when your own words fail. They give voice to every human emotion: joy, despair, anger, gratitude, bewilderment, praise. Each one directed toward God.

The East divides the Psalter into twenty sections called kathismata, each assigned to particular days and services. This ensures that the entire range of human experience before God is traversed regularly. No psalm is skipped because it's uncomfortable. The cursing psalms, the despairing psalms, the psalms of rage: all are prayed, because all of human life belongs before God, not just the polished parts.

The Horologion

The Horologion, the "Book of Hours," is the structural framework of the Eastern Church's daily prayer cycle. It divides the day into services: Vespers (evening), Compline (after supper), Midnight Office, Matins (morning), and the Hours (First, Third, Sixth, Ninth). Together, these services sanctify every portion of the day, turning clock time into sacred time.

In parishes, not all of these services are celebrated daily. That level of observance belongs primarily to monasteries. But the structure exists as an ideal, and many laypeople incorporate elements of the Hours into their daily rule. The Third Hour (roughly 9 AM) recalls the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. The Sixth Hour (noon) recalls the Crucifixion. The Ninth Hour (3 PM) recalls Christ's death. To pause at these moments, even briefly, is to anchor the day in the events of salvation.

Prayer is the test of everything; prayer is also the source of everything; prayer is the driving force of everything; prayer is also the director of everything. If prayer is right, everything is right.
— St. Theophan the Recluse, 19th-century Russian spiritual writer

The Akathist

Among the treasures of Eastern prayer are the Akathists, extended hymns of praise sung while standing (the word literally means "not sitting"). The most famous is the Akathist to the Theotokos, a magnificent 6th-century hymn that weaves together Scripture, theology, and poetry in praise of the Mother of God. Akathists have also been composed for Christ, for saints, for particular mysteries of the faith, and they remain a beloved form of popular devotion.

An akathist follows a fixed structure: thirteen kontakia (short hymns ending in "Alleluia") alternating with thirteen ikoi (longer hymns with a series of "Rejoice" acclamations). The effect is cumulative. As the hymn unfolds, the reader is drawn deeper into contemplation of its subject. Many Eastern Christians add an akathist to their daily rule, particularly during feast seasons.

Hesychasm — The Prayer of Stillness

The deepest current of Eastern prayer tradition is hesychasm, from the Greek hesychia, meaning stillness, quiet, inner peace. Hesychasm is not a technique. It is not Eastern Christianity's version of meditation. It is a disposition of the whole person, body, mind, and spirit, oriented toward encounter with the living God.

The hesychast tradition teaches that through sustained practice of the Jesus Prayer, combined with fasting, watchfulness, and the guidance of a spiritual father, the Christian can experience the uncreated light of God. The same light that shone on Mount Tabor at Christ's Transfiguration. Not metaphor. The great hesychast theologian St. Gregory Palamas (14th century) insisted that this light is God Himself, His energies, not His essence, and that this experience is available to every baptized Christian, not only to monks.

Hesychasm was not without controversy. Critics accused the hesychasts of navel-gazing (literally; some monks adopted a physical posture with the chin on the chest). But the Church vindicated Palamas at the councils of 1341 and 1351, and hesychasm has since been recognized as the authentic heart of Orthodox and Eastern Catholic spirituality. Not an esoteric practice for spiritual athletes. The natural flowering of the prayer life that begins with "Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me."

The Philokalia, a massive collection of Eastern spiritual texts compiled in the eighteenth century, is the essential sourcebook for hesychast prayer. It is demanding but inexhaustible.

Prayer as Relationship

Eastern Christianity insists that prayer is not a monologue. It is a conversation with a Person. The Jesus Prayer addresses Jesus. The psalms are spoken to God. The akathists praise a living Mother who hears and responds. The East has no patience for prayer understood as self-improvement, stress reduction, or mindfulness technique. These may be side effects. They are not the point.

The point is communion. The God who became human in a stable in Bethlehem still desires to enter the stable of the human heart, cluttered and inadequate as it is. Prayer is opening the door. Quite literally, learning to breathe: learning to inhale the mercy of God and exhale the gratitude that is the only fitting response.
