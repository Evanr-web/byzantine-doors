East and West, Catholic and Orthodox

One tradition, painful divisions, and the hope for unity.

For the first thousand years of Christianity, East and West were one Church. They spoke different languages, Greek in the East, Latin in the West. They developed different liturgical traditions, different theological vocabularies, different approaches to church governance. But they shared the same faith, the same sacraments, the same creed. Bishops of Rome and patriarchs of Constantinople disagreed, sometimes bitterly, but they remained in communion.

They were family. A family that argued, but a family nonetheless.

Then, in 1054, something broke. Or rather, something that had been cracking for centuries finally gave way. The result was the Great Schism, the division of Christendom into the Roman Catholic West and the Eastern Orthodox East. Nearly a thousand years later, the wound has not fully healed. Understanding what happened, and why, is essential for anyone seeking to understand Eastern Christianity, because the division is not merely historical. It is a living reality that shapes the identity of every Eastern Christian today.

What Actually Happened in 1054

The popular version: in July 1054, Cardinal Humbert of Silva Candida marched into the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople and slapped a papal bull of excommunication on the altar. Patriarch Michael Cerularius responded by excommunicating the Roman legates. And just like that, the Church was split in two.

The reality is far more complicated. The excommunications of 1054 were directed at individuals, not at entire churches. Most Christians at the time barely noticed. The real separation happened gradually, over centuries, driven by a combination of theological disagreements, political rivalries, cultural differences, and wounded pride on both sides.

The tensions had been building for a long time. As early as the 9th century, Patriarch Photius of Constantinople and Pope Nicholas I clashed over jurisdiction and over the Western addition to the Creed. The growing political divergence between the Byzantine Empire and the Frankish West created an environment in which theological differences were amplified by cultural suspicion. East and West were drifting apart long before anyone put pen to excommunication.

The separation between East and West was not a single dramatic event but a long process of estrangement — a gradual cooling of charity until the bonds of communion could no longer hold.
— Yves Congar, After Nine Hundred Years, 1959

The Filioque

The most famous theological disagreement concerns a single Latin word: filioque, "and the Son." The original Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, as affirmed by the ecumenical councils of 325 and 381, states that the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." At some point in the early medieval West, probably in Visigothic Spain, the phrase "and the Son" was added, so that the Creed read "proceeds from the Father and the Son."

The addition was made without the consent of an ecumenical council. This troubled the East deeply, not only because of what the addition says, but because of how it was made. The Creed belongs to the whole Church; no local church has the authority to alter it unilaterally.

The theological question, does the Spirit proceed from the Father alone or from the Father and the Son, is genuinely complex, and serious theologians on both sides have argued for centuries that the differences may be more linguistic than substantive. But the procedural question, who has the authority to change the Creed, remains a live issue.

Many Western theologians today acknowledge that the original Creed should not have been altered without conciliar authority. Pope Benedict XVI recited the Creed without the filioque on several occasions when celebrating with Eastern Christians. The question is far from resolved, but there is more convergence than is commonly assumed.

Papal Primacy

The question of the Pope's role is perhaps the deepest structural difference between East and West. Both sides agree that the Bishop of Rome held a place of honor among the ancient patriarchs. Rome was the city of Peter and Paul, and its bishop was acknowledged as "first among equals." But what does "first among equals" mean in practice?

The West gradually developed a theology of papal primacy that extended far beyond a primacy of honor. By the time of the First Vatican Council (1870), the Roman Catholic Church had defined papal infallibility: the doctrine that the Pope, when speaking ex cathedra on matters of faith and morals, is preserved from error by the Holy Spirit.

The East finds this doctrine impossible to accept. In the Eastern understanding, infallibility belongs to the Church as a whole, expressed through ecumenical councils, not to any single bishop, however venerable his see. The question is not whether Peter held a special place among the apostles (the East readily acknowledges this) but whether Peter's successor inherits a jurisdiction over the entire Church that can override the authority of other bishops, synods, and councils.

The East says no. The West, at least in its official teaching, says yes. Though recent Catholic theology has shown increasing interest in distinguishing between different levels of papal authority and in recovering a more synodal model of governance.

The Crusades and Their Lasting Wound

Theological differences might have been negotiated. What made reconciliation immeasurably harder was the Fourth Crusade of 1204.

Western Crusaders, diverted from their original mission to the Holy Land, sacked Constantinople. A Christian city. The greatest Christian city in the world. They looted churches, desecrated altars, installed a Latin patriarch, and carved the Byzantine Empire into feudal fiefdoms.

The sack of Constantinople was not merely a political disaster; it was a spiritual trauma that the East has never fully recovered from. For the Orthodox, it confirmed their worst suspicions about the West: that Rome was not merely different but hostile, not merely mistaken but aggressive. Eight centuries later, the memory still burns. Any serious ecumenical effort must reckon with this wound honestly.

Pope John Paul II formally apologized for the Fourth Crusade in 2004, calling it a source of "deep regret." Patriarch Bartholomew accepted the apology. Small steps, but real ones.

I insistently pray the Holy Spirit to shine His light upon us, enlightening all the pastors and theologians of our churches, that we may seek — together — the forms in which this ministry of the Bishop of Rome may accomplish a service of love recognized by all.
— Pope John Paul II, Ut Unum Sint, 1995

What Byzantine Catholics Are

And here is where this website's particular story comes in.

Byzantine Catholics are Eastern Christians, in liturgy, theology, spirituality, and tradition, who are in full communion with the Bishop of Rome. They celebrate the Divine Liturgy, not the Roman Mass. They baptize by immersion, chrismate infants, and commune babies. Their priests can be married. Their churches have iconostases. Their theology speaks of theosis, not merely forgiveness. In almost every visible respect, they are indistinguishable from their Orthodox counterparts.

But they accept the Pope as the successor of Peter and the visible center of Christian unity. This does not mean they have adopted Western theology wholesale. They maintain their own theological tradition, their own canon law, their own spiritual patrimony. The Second Vatican Council explicitly affirmed that Eastern Catholic churches have a "right and duty" to preserve their own traditions and that "Latinization" is to be avoided.

The existence of Byzantine Catholics is sometimes a source of tension. Some Orthodox view them as a Trojan horse, Eastern Christians co-opted by Rome. Some Latin Catholics view them as exotic novelties. Byzantine Catholics themselves often feel caught between worlds, too Eastern for the West, too Roman for the East. But they also occupy a unique position: living proof that Eastern and Western Christianity can coexist within a single communion, that unity does not require uniformity.

The Hope for Unity

The division between East and West is real, and it would be dishonest to minimize it. There are genuine theological differences that cannot be dissolved by good will alone. But there is also genuine hope. The mutual excommunications of 1054 were formally lifted in 1964 by Pope Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras. Theological dialogues continue. Personal relationships between Catholic and Orthodox leaders have never been warmer.

The path to unity will be long. It may require new theological categories, new models of governance, and a willingness on both sides to distinguish between what is essential and what is merely customary. The goal is not absorption of one tradition by the other. The goal is the restoration of the communion that once existed, a communion that honored diversity within unity, that allowed Rome and Constantinople and Alexandria and Antioch and Jerusalem to be genuinely different while remaining genuinely one.

That goal is worth praying for. Worth working for. And worth believing in, because the Christ who prayed "that they may all be one" is not in the habit of praying for impossible things.
