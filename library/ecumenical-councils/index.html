The Seven Ecumenical Councils

From Nicaea to Nicaea — seven gatherings that defined Christian doctrine, settled the great christological debates, and vindicated the icons.

Between 325 and 787, the undivided Church convened seven great councils. Each one responded to a crisis. Each one clarified something essential about who Christ is, what salvation means, and how Christians worship. These were not academic conferences. Bishops traveled weeks by horseback or ship. Emperors intervened. Tempers flared. People were exiled. The stakes were ultimate: get the theology wrong, and the entire structure of Christian hope collapses.

The Eastern Churches call these the "Seven Ecumenical Councils" and regard their definitions as irreformable. Not because bishops are infallible, but because the Holy Spirit, working through the consensus of the whole Church, protected these particular decisions from error. Understanding the councils is understanding the grammar of Christian faith.

Nicaea I (325): Is the Son God?

A priest in Alexandria named Arius taught that the Son of God was a creature. Exalted, first-created, godlike — but not God. "There was a time when He was not," Arius said.

Emperor Constantine, newly converted and alarmed by a Church tearing itself apart, summoned bishops to the city of Nicaea. Around 318 came. The council's answer was blunt: the Son is homoousios — "of one substance" — with the Father. Not similar. Not analogous. The same divine reality. The Nicene Creed, still recited in every liturgy, was born here.

Arius lost. But Arianism did not die at Nicaea. It took another sixty years of political turmoil, imperial flip-flopping, and the theological genius of the Cappadocian Fathers to settle the question for good.

Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten not made, of one substance with the Father.
— The Nicene Creed, 325 / 381

Constantinople I (381): And the Holy Spirit?

If the Son is God, what about the Spirit? A group called the Pneumatomachoi ("Spirit-fighters") said the Spirit was a creature, a servant, a force — anything but divine. The council at Constantinople settled it. The Creed was expanded: the Spirit "proceeds from the Father," is "worshipped and glorified" together with Father and Son, and "spoke through the prophets."

This is the Creed as the Eastern Churches still confess it. The later Western addition of "and the Son" (the filioque) was not part of this council's decision — a fact that would become enormously consequential.

Ephesus (431): Can Mary Be Called "Mother of God"?

Nestorius, Patriarch of Constantinople, refused to call Mary Theotokos — "God-bearer" or "Mother of God." He preferred Christotokos: mother of Christ. The distinction sounds pedantic. It was not.

If Mary bore only the human nature of Christ, then Christ's humanity and divinity are separable. Two persons wearing one suit. The council at Ephesus, led fiercely by Cyril of Alexandria, declared: Mary is Theotokos because the child she bore is one person, and that person is God. The title protects Christology, not Mariology. It says something about Christ first and about Mary second.

Chalcedon (451): One Person, Two Natures

The christological question came to a head at Chalcedon. If Christ is truly God and truly human, how do those two natures relate? Eutyches taught that the two natures merged into one — the human absorbed into the divine like a drop of wine in the ocean. The council rejected this. It also rejected Nestorius's separation.

The Chalcedonian Definition declared that Christ exists "in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation." Four negatives. The council told the Church what not to say rather than constructing a tidy philosophical system. The mystery remains. But the guardrails are clear.

Chalcedon split the Church. The Oriental Orthodox (Coptic, Armenian, Syriac, Ethiopian) rejected its language, though modern dialogue has shown the disagreement may have been more verbal than substantive. The wound is still healing.

Constantinople II (553): Cleaning Up

Emperor Justinian convened this council partly to reconcile the opponents of Chalcedon by condemning three theologians (the "Three Chapters") whose writings seemed too Nestorian. The politics were messy. Pope Vigilius was in Constantinople, under imperial pressure, and his relationship with the council was contentious.

The theological contribution: a stronger emphasis on the unity of Christ's person. The Theopaschite formula — "one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh" — was affirmed. God did not merely watch the Crucifixion from a safe distance. God experienced it.

Constantinople III (680–681): Does Christ Have a Human Will?

The Monothelites said Christ had only one will: the divine will. This seemed to simplify things. It actually gutted the Incarnation. If Christ has no human will, His humanity is incomplete. He did not struggle in Gethsemane. He did not choose obedience. He was a puppet.

The council declared two wills in Christ, divine and human, operating in harmony without conflict. The human will freely submits to the divine will — not by compulsion, but by love. This matters for theosis: if Christ's human will was real, then human will can genuinely cooperate with God. Freedom is not abolished by grace. It is fulfilled.

Nicaea II (787): The Icons

The Iconoclast emperors had spent decades smashing icons, whitewashing churches, and persecuting icon-venerators. The theological argument: icons of Christ violate the commandment against graven images. They are idolatry.

Nicaea II answered with a distinction and a christological argument. The distinction: latreia (worship) belongs to God alone; proskynesis (veneration, honor) may be given to icons, the Cross, the saints, and the relics. Venerating an icon is not worshipping the wood and paint. The honor passes to the person depicted.

The christological argument: because God became visible in Christ, Christ can be depicted. To forbid images of Christ is to deny the Incarnation. If God took on flesh, that flesh can be painted. The icon is a witness to the reality of the Incarnation.

Nicaea II closed the age of the councils. The icon had been vindicated. Every year, on the first Sunday of Great Lent, the Eastern Churches celebrate the "Triumph of Orthodoxy" — a feast commemorating this victory.

I do not worship matter. I worship the God of matter, who became matter for my sake, who willed to take His abode in matter, who worked out my salvation through matter.
— St. John of Damascus, On the Divine Images, First Apology (Louth translation)

Why They Still Matter

These seven councils built the doctrinal architecture that Eastern and Western Christians still inhabit. The Creed recited at every Divine Liturgy is their product. The icons on the iconostasis are their vindication. The theological vocabulary used in every seminary and catechism class — homoousios, Theotokos, two natures, two wills — was forged in these debates.

More than that, the councils established a model for how the Church resolves disputes: not by papal decree alone, not by individual theologians, but by the gathered bishops of the whole Church, guided (the East believes) by the Holy Spirit. This conciliar model remains the Eastern understanding of authority. The Church is not a monarchy. It is a communion.

Further Reading

For accessible introductions, start with Kallistos Ware's The Orthodox Church (chapters on the early councils) or Jaroslav Pelikan's The Christian Tradition, Vol. 1: The Emergence of the Catholic Tradition. For the Chalcedonian debates specifically, John Meyendorff's Christ in Eastern Christian Thought remains essential. Leo Donald Davis's The First Seven Ecumenical Councils provides a readable, council-by-council narrative.
