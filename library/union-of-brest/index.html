The Union of Brest (1596)

How Eastern Christians in Ukraine chose communion with Rome while keeping their Byzantine heritage — a decision still shaping the Church today.

Home › Library › The Union of Brest (1596)
Category History
Read time 7 min read

In October 1596, a group of Ruthenian Orthodox bishops gathered in the city of Brest (in modern-day Belarus) and declared their union with the Bishop of Rome. They kept their Byzantine liturgy. They kept their married clergy. They kept their theology, their icons, their chant. What changed was their ecclesiastical allegiance: they now recognized the Pope as the visible head of the universal Church.

This was the Union of Brest, and its consequences have rippled through four centuries of Eastern European history. It created the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church — one of the largest Eastern Catholic communities in the world. It also created a wound between Eastern Catholics and Orthodox that has never fully healed.

The Context

To understand Brest, you need to understand the situation of Orthodoxy in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth at the end of the sixteenth century.

The Commonwealth was a Catholic state. Latin-rite Catholicism was the religion of the ruling class, the nobility, and the royal court. The Orthodox Ruthenians (ancestors of modern Ukrainians and Belarusians) were second-class citizens. Their bishops had less political standing than Catholic ones. Their Church was poorly organized, underfunded, and increasingly marginalized.

Meanwhile, the Orthodox Patriarchate of Constantinople — the spiritual authority the Ruthenian Church looked to — was under Ottoman occupation and largely unable to provide effective leadership. The Ruthenian bishops felt abandoned: too far from Constantinople to get help, too subordinate in the Commonwealth to get respect.

Rome offered a solution. If the Ruthenian bishops accepted papal primacy, they would receive equal standing with Latin-rite bishops in the Commonwealth. They could keep everything else: liturgy, canon law, married clergy, theological traditions. All of it.

For bishops struggling with political marginalization and institutional decay, the offer was compelling.

We seek nothing but the good of our Church and our people. We preserve our rite, our customs, our traditions. We add only the acknowledgment of one head of the universal Church.
— Metropolitan Ipatiy Potiy, one of the architects of the Union, 1595

What Happened at Brest

Two synods met in Brest simultaneously. One, led by Metropolitan Michael Rohoza and several bishops, ratified the union with Rome. The other, led by bishops who opposed the union and supported by Prince Konstanty Ostrogski (the most powerful Orthodox nobleman in the Commonwealth), rejected it and excommunicated the pro-union bishops.

The split was immediate and bitter. Families divided. Parishes divided. The same church building could become a battleground between pro-union and anti-union factions. The Polish crown backed the union and transferred Orthodox properties to the Greek-Catholic bishops, deepening resentment.

For the opponents, the union was a betrayal. It fractured an already weakened Church and handed its leadership to Rome. For the supporters, it was a survival strategy: the only way to preserve the Byzantine tradition within a hostile political environment.

Both sides had a point.

The Promises and the Reality

Rome had promised that the Greek-Catholic Church would retain its full Byzantine identity. In practice, the promise was unevenly kept.

Over the following centuries, a process called Latinization gradually eroded Eastern distinctiveness. Latin devotional practices crept in: rosaries, Stations of the Cross, Benediction of the Blessed Sacrament, Corpus Christi processions. Church architecture shifted toward Western models. Some Greek-Catholic seminaries taught Latin theology rather than Eastern patristics. The iconostasis shrank or disappeared in some parishes, replaced by statues and side altars.

This was not a deliberate conspiracy. It was the natural result of an Eastern minority living within a Latin majority. The stronger culture absorbed the weaker one, slowly, through proximity and imitation. But for those who valued the Byzantine heritage, the erosion was painful.

The twentieth century brought a correction. The liturgical movement, the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965), and a renewed emphasis on the Eastern Churches' own patrimony led to a deliberate effort to recover what had been lost. Vatican II's decree Orientalium Ecclesiarum urged the Eastern Catholic Churches to return to their authentic traditions. The process is ongoing.

The Twentieth Century: Suppression and Survival

The Greek-Catholic Church's darkest chapter came under Soviet rule. In 1946, at a rigged synod in Lviv, the Soviet authorities forced the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church to "reunite" with the Russian Orthodox Church. Bishops who refused were arrested. Priests were sent to the gulag. The Church was officially dissolved.

It went underground. For over forty years, the largest banned religious community in the world maintained its life in secret: clandestine liturgies in apartments, ordinations in labor camps, catechesis whispered to children. When Ukraine gained independence in 1991, the Greek-Catholic Church emerged from the catacombs. It had survived.

The experience of persecution profoundly shaped the Church's identity. The Greek-Catholics of Ukraine understand themselves as a community that has paid for its faith in blood. This is not distant history. There are living people who attended underground liturgies as children. The memory is fresh.

I come from a Church of martyrs.
— Patriarch Josyf Slipyj, after 18 years in Soviet prison camps, upon his release in 1963

Where Things Stand

Today the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church (UGCC) is the largest Eastern Catholic Church in the world, with around five million faithful. It is headquartered in Kyiv and led by a Major Archbishop (currently Sviatoslav Shevchuk). It operates seminaries, universities, charitable organizations, and parishes across Ukraine, Europe, and the diaspora in North America and Australia.

The relationship with Orthodoxy remains complex. The Russian Orthodox Church regards Eastern Catholics with deep suspicion, viewing the Union of Brest as a historical injustice and the Greek-Catholic Church as a "Trojan horse" for Roman influence in the East. The Ecumenical Patriarchate and other Orthodox Churches have varying perspectives, but the issue remains one of the most sensitive in ecumenical dialogue.

Within the UGCC itself, there is a vibrant movement to recover authentic Byzantine identity: restoring iconography, reviving traditional chant, studying the Eastern Fathers, and resisting further Latinization. At the same time, there is a healthy recognition that four centuries of communion with Rome have produced their own legitimate developments. The Church is not trying to become Orthodox. It is trying to be fully itself: Eastern in soul, Catholic in communion.

An Irenic Conclusion

The Union of Brest was not a simple event with simple morals. The bishops who chose Rome were not traitors. The bishops who refused were not reactionaries. Both were trying to preserve the faith and protect their people in an impossible situation.

The lesson of Brest is not about which side was right. It is about the cost of division. When the Body of Christ is fractured, every attempt to mend it creates new fractures. The only way forward is the hard, slow, patient work of dialogue, mutual respect, and shared prayer.

The Greek-Catholic tradition at its best holds a profound hope: that East and West can coexist within one communion, each enriching the other, neither absorbing the other. Whether that hope is realistic remains to be seen. That it is worth pursuing seems beyond question.

Further Reading

Borys Gudziak's Crisis and Reform: The Kyivan Metropolitanate, the Patriarchate of Constantinople, and the Genesis of the Union of Brest is the definitive academic study of the Union's origins. For the Soviet persecution, see Serge Keleher's Passion and Resurrection: The Greek Catholic Church in Soviet Ukraine. The catechism Christ Our Pascha, published by the UGCC, provides the Church's own self-understanding. Kallistos Ware's The Orthodox Church gives the Orthodox perspective on the Union.
