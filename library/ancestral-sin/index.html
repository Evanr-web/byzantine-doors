Ancestral Sin vs. Original Sin

Eastern Christianity doesn't teach inherited guilt. It teaches inherited mortality — and the difference reshapes everything.

Home › Library › Ancestral Sin vs. Original Sin
Category Theology
Read time 6 min read

Ask a Western Christian what happened in the Garden of Eden and the answer will probably involve guilt. Adam sinned. That guilt passed to every human being. We are born guilty, stained, condemned before we draw our first breath. This is the doctrine of Original Sin as developed by St. Augustine of Hippo in the fifth century, and it became the cornerstone of Western soteriology — the entire theory of how salvation works.

The East never adopted it.

Eastern Christianity teaches something different: ancestral sin (propatirikon amartima in Greek). The distinction is not a minor footnote. It produces a fundamentally different understanding of human nature, of baptism, of salvation, and of God.

What Augustine Taught

Augustine was brilliant, tortured, and enormously influential. His reading of Romans 5:12 — "in whom all sinned" — led him to conclude that Adam's guilt was transmitted biologically to every descendant. Humanity is a massa damnata, a "condemned lump." Infants who die unbaptized face damnation (or at best, limbo). Human nature after the Fall is not merely weakened. It is corrupt, incapable of choosing good without special grace.

This framework drove Western theology for a millennium and a half. It shaped the Reformation. Luther and Calvin intensified Augustine's pessimism. The Catholic Church moderated it but kept the essential structure. The entire Western conversation about predestination, free will, and grace presupposes Augustinian categories.

The Eastern Fathers read the same Scripture. They reached different conclusions.

Each person is punished for their own sins, not for the sin of their ancestor.
— St. John Chrysostom, Homilies on Romans, 4th century

What the East Teaches

The Greek Fathers — John Chrysostom, Gregory of Nyssa, Maximus the Confessor — read Romans 5:12 differently. The crucial phrase, eph' hō pantes hēmarton, they translated as "because of which all sinned" rather than "in whom all sinned." The difference is everything.

In the Eastern reading, Adam's sin introduced death into the world. Death, once present, distorted human existence. Because humans are mortal, they are afraid. Because they are afraid, they grasp, hoard, compete, and sin. Sin is not inherited as guilt. Sin is a consequence of mortality. Humans sin because they are dying, not the other way around.

Put sharply: the West says humans die because they sin. The East says humans sin because they die.

The Image of God: Damaged, Not Destroyed

This distinction has enormous consequences for how each tradition views human nature. In the Augustinian framework, the Fall shattered the imago Dei. Human nature is so corrupted that without prevenient grace, a person cannot even desire God. The will is bound.

The Eastern Fathers disagree. The image of God in humanity is darkened but not destroyed. Think of a gold coin dropped in mud. The mud obscures the image, but the gold is still gold. The image is still there, waiting to be cleaned and restored. Human beings retain the capacity to respond to God, to choose, to cooperate with grace. The will is weakened. It is not obliterated.

This is why the Eastern tradition speaks of salvation as healing rather than acquittal. Humanity is not a criminal standing before a judge. Humanity is a patient lying in a hospital. The Church is the hospital. The sacraments are the medicine. The goal is not a verdict of "not guilty" but a restoration to health — to the full, radiant humanity that God intended from the beginning.

Baptism: What Changes?

In the Augustinian West, infant baptism primarily removes the stain of original sin. Without baptism, the infant remains under condemnation.

In the East, infant baptism does something different. It grafts the child into the Body of Christ. It begins the process of theosis. It gives the child access to the Eucharist (Eastern infants receive Communion immediately after baptism and chrismation). The emphasis is not on removing guilt but on beginning a life of transformation within the Church.

Eastern theology has never needed the concept of limbo. Unbaptized infants are entrusted to the mercy of God without anxiety. The tradition does not speculate about their damnation because it does not believe they are born condemned.

Free Will and Cooperation

Perhaps the sharpest practical difference: the Eastern tradition insists on synergy — genuine human cooperation with divine grace. Salvation is not something done to a passive recipient. It is a dance, a partnership. God initiates. The human person responds. Neither can do it alone.

This is not Pelagianism (the heresy that humans can save themselves). The East has always taught that grace is primary, that God makes the first move, that without the Holy Spirit no genuine spiritual progress is possible. But grace does not override the will. It empowers it. It heals it. It invites it. The human "yes" matters.

The Western debates about predestination — the agonized arguments between Calvinists and Arminians, Jansenists and Molinists — are largely absent from Eastern theology. Not because the East has solved the problem, but because it starts from different premises. If human nature is damaged but not destroyed, if the will is weakened but not dead, then the entire framework of irresistible grace and unconditional election becomes unnecessary.

The human being, even after the fall, retains the image of God — darkened, wounded, but not destroyed. The likeness of God is the goal toward which the human person strives through the cooperation of free will and divine grace.
— Christ Our Pascha, Catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church, §157

An Irenic Note

This article is not a polemic against Augustine or the Western tradition. Augustine was a saint — recognized as such by East and West alike. His insights into grace, interiority, and the restless human heart remain profound. Many Western theologians today are recovering a more nuanced reading of Augustine, and the Catholic Church's own teaching has moved well beyond the harshest Augustinian positions.

The point is not that the West is wrong and the East is right. The point is that the Eastern tradition offers a different lens — one that many Western Christians find liberating when they encounter it. A God who heals rather than condemns. A human nature that is sick rather than depraved. A salvation that involves the whole person, body and soul, in a cooperative journey toward the divine life.

The Anastasis icon says it all. Christ does not stand at the gates of Hades with a clipboard, checking who deserves release. He descends. He breaks the gates. He grabs Adam and Eve by the wrists and hauls them out. That is the Eastern Gospel.

Further Reading

John Meyendorff's Byzantine Theology (chapter on "Man") provides the clearest academic treatment of the Eastern view. For a catechetical approach, see Christ Our Pascha, the catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church, §§ 149–165. Kallistos Ware discusses the distinction in The Orthodox Way, and Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church offers a more advanced perspective.
