The Iconostasis Explained

The wall of icons that separates nave from sanctuary — every icon has a place, every placement carries meaning, and the Royal Doors open onto eternity.

Walk into a Byzantine church and the first thing that commands your attention is the wall. Not the walls of the building. The wall: a screen of icons stretching across the front of the church, separating the nave (where the congregation stands) from the sanctuary (where the altar stands). This is the iconostasis — from the Greek eikon (image) and stasis (standing place). A place where images stand.

Visitors from Western traditions sometimes see the iconostasis as a barrier. Something blocking the view. Hiding the action. The Eastern understanding is the opposite. The iconostasis does not hide the sacred. It reveals it. It is a window, not a wall. A theology in wood and paint.

Origins

The earliest Christian churches had no iconostasis. The sanctuary was separated from the nave by a low barrier called a templon — waist-high, often made of marble, with columns supporting a beam (epistyle) across the top. You could see the altar. The separation was symbolic, not visual.

Over the centuries, icons began to accumulate on and around the templon. First a few. Then rows. By the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, especially in the Russian tradition, the iconostasis had grown into a towering structure of five or more tiers, filling the entire east wall of the church from floor to ceiling.

The development was organic, not decreed. No council mandated the full iconostasis. It grew from the instincts of worship: if icons are windows into heaven, then multiplying them creates an entire wall of heaven, visible and present.

The Royal Doors

At the center of the iconostasis stand the Royal Doors (also called the Holy Doors or Beautiful Gates). These are the double doors through which the priest enters and exits the sanctuary during the liturgy. They are called "royal" because through them comes the King — Christ Himself, in the Eucharist.

The Royal Doors typically bear icons of the four Evangelists (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) and the Annunciation (the Archangel Gabriel and the Virgin Mary). The logic: the Gospels are the door through which Christ enters human consciousness, and the Annunciation is the door through which He entered human flesh.

During the Divine Liturgy, the opening and closing of the Royal Doors marks the rhythm of revelation and mystery. When they open, heaven and earth communicate visibly. When they close, the mystery continues behind the veil. The congregation sees and does not see, knows and does not know. This tension is intentional.

The Bottom Tier: The Local Row

The lowest tier of icons, at eye level, is the most important for the worshipper. Its arrangement follows a consistent pattern across nearly all Byzantine churches:

To the right of the Royal Doors (as you face the iconostasis): the icon of Christ. Almost always Christ Pantocrator — the "Ruler of All" — holding the Gospels in one hand and blessing with the other. This is His church. He is the first face you see.

To the left of the Royal Doors: the icon of the Theotokos (the Mother of God), usually holding the Christ Child. She points toward Him. Her presence says: God was born. He has a mother. The Incarnation is real.

To the right of Christ: the icon of St. John the Baptist, the Forerunner — the last prophet, the one who prepared the way.

To the left of the Theotokos: the patronal icon — the icon of the saint or feast to whom the church is dedicated. A church named Holy Transfiguration will have the Transfiguration icon here. A church named St. Nicholas will have St. Nicholas.

I do not worship matter. I worship the God of matter, who became matter for my sake, who willed to take His abode in matter, who worked out my salvation through matter. I will not cease from honouring that matter which works for my salvation.
— St. John of Damascus, On the Divine Images, First Apology (Louth translation)

The Upper Tiers

Above the local row, larger iconostases add additional tiers. The arrangement varies by tradition, but the most common pattern in the Russian and Ukrainian traditions includes:

The Deesis tier: Christ enthroned at center, flanked by the Theotokos and John the Baptist, who turn toward Him in intercession. Behind them, archangels, apostles, and major saints, all facing Christ. This is the Church praying — a visual representation of the communion of saints.

The Feast tier: Icons of the twelve great feasts of the liturgical year: Nativity, Theophany, Transfiguration, Crucifixion, Resurrection, Ascension, Pentecost, and others. The cycle of salvation history, compressed into a single row.

The Prophets tier: Old Testament prophets holding scrolls with their messianic prophecies. Moses, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Elijah. At the center, the icon of the Theotokos of the Sign — Mary with arms raised in prayer, the Christ Child visible in her womb. She is the fulfillment of everything the prophets foretold.

The Patriarchs tier: The ancestors of the faith: Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Noah. At the very top, sometimes, the icon of the Holy Trinity. The whole history of salvation, from creation to the Second Coming, read from top to bottom.

The Side Doors

Flanking the Royal Doors are two smaller doors, one on each side. These are the deacon's doors (or "north" and "south" doors). Through them, deacons, servers, and clergy move in and out of the sanctuary during services. They often bear icons of the Archangels Michael and Gabriel, or of the deacon-saints Stephen and Laurence.

The arrangement of three doors in the iconostasis is sometimes read as trinitarian, sometimes as a reference to the three entrances of the ancient Temple in Jerusalem. Both readings coexist.

Not a Wall — A Window

The iconostasis does not separate the congregation from God. It makes visible what is otherwise invisible: the communion of saints, the presence of Christ, the intercession of the Theotokos, the sweep of salvation history from Adam to the Second Coming.

Stand in front of an iconostasis and look. The faces look back. They are not decorations. They are presences. The saints depicted are understood to be genuinely present in the liturgy, praying alongside the congregation. The iconostasis makes that invisible reality visible.

The Royal Doors open. Incense billows out. The priest emerges carrying the Gospel or the Eucharist. For a moment, the boundary between heaven and earth dissolves. Then the doors close again. The mystery continues.

That rhythm — revelation and hiddenness, presence and mystery — is the heartbeat of Byzantine worship. The iconostasis is its visible expression.

Further Reading

For a theological treatment of icons and their place in worship, see Leonid Ouspensky's Theology of the Icon. Solrunn Nes's The Mystical Language of Icons provides an accessible visual introduction. For the historical development of the iconostasis as an architectural form, see Sharon Gerstel's Beholding the Sacred Mysteries.
