The Great Schism of 1054

What actually happened, what didn't, and why healing this wound matters more than assigning blame.

Category Ecumenism
Read time 7 min read

On July 16, 1054, Cardinal Humbert of Silva Candida strode into the Hagia Sophia during the Saturday afternoon liturgy and placed a papal bull of excommunication on the high altar. Then he walked out. Patriarch Michael Cerularius of Constantinople picked it up, read it, and excommunicated Humbert right back.

That is the popular version: one dramatic afternoon, two stubborn men, and Christianity split in half. The reality is messier, slower, and more instructive. The "Great Schism" was not a single event. It was a process that took centuries, and in some ways it is still unfolding.

What Actually Happened in 1054

Humbert was in Constantinople on a diplomatic mission from Pope Leo IX. The relationship between Rome and Constantinople had been deteriorating for decades over multiple issues: jurisdictional disputes in southern Italy, liturgical differences, and the filioque controversy. When Pope Leo died during Humbert's visit, the cardinal's authority to act on papal behalf became legally questionable.

Humbert's bull excommunicated Cerularius and his supporters. Cerularius's synod excommunicated Humbert and the other legates. Neither side excommunicated the other's entire church. The mutual excommunications were personal and limited. Most Christians in both East and West barely noticed.

For decades afterward, communion between Rome and Constantinople continued in many places. Pilgrims traveled freely. Clergy concelebrated. The "schism of 1054" became a fixed date in textbooks only much later, when historians needed a clean turning point for a story that resists one.

The Deeper Causes

The real schism grew from roots far older than 1054.

Language

The Roman West spoke Latin. The Byzantine East spoke Greek. By the ninth century, educated churchmen in each half could no longer read the other's theology in the original. Misunderstandings multiplied. Subtle distinctions in one language became heresies in translation.

Ecclesiology

Rome understood the Pope as the supreme head of the entire Church, with universal jurisdiction. Constantinople understood the Patriarch of Rome as "first among equals" — holding a primacy of honor and a certain appellate authority, but not the right to intervene directly in other patriarchates. This disagreement was not about personalities. It was about the fundamental structure of the Church.

Both sides could cite early evidence for their positions. The argument is still unresolved.

The Filioque

The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed (381) says the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." Sometime in the sixth or seventh century, churches in Spain began adding filioque — "and the Son." The addition spread through the Frankish kingdoms and eventually reached Rome. By 1014, the Pope was reciting the Creed with the filioque at Mass.

The East objected on two grounds. First: the Creed was defined by an ecumenical council, and no single church has the authority to alter it unilaterally. Second: the theology itself is problematic, potentially subordinating the Spirit and distorting the inner life of the Trinity. (For more, see the article on the Filioque.)

Culture and Politics

The coronation of Charlemagne as "Emperor of the Romans" in 800 infuriated Constantinople. There was already a Roman Emperor, and he lived in Constantinople. The Frankish claim created a rival political center with its own theological agenda.

The Norman conquest of Byzantine southern Italy in the eleventh century forced Latin practices on Greek Christians. The Crusades — especially the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople in 1204 — turned theological tension into bitter, enduring resentment.

Even the Saracens would have been more merciful.
— Nicetas Choniates, Byzantine historian, on the Fourth Crusade, 1204

1204: The Real Breaking Point

If 1054 was a diplomatic spat, 1204 was a catastrophe. The armies of the Fourth Crusade, originally bound for the Holy Land, were diverted to Constantinople by Venetian commercial interests and Byzantine dynastic politics. They sacked the city. For three days, Western soldiers looted churches, smashed icons, stole relics, assaulted civilians, and installed a Latin patriarch in the Hagia Sophia.

The theological disagreements of the previous centuries became personal. Families had been killed. Churches had been desecrated. The memory of 1204 burns in Eastern Christian consciousness to this day. When Pope John Paul II visited Greece in 2001, he apologized for the Fourth Crusade. The gesture was significant. The wound has not fully closed.

Failed Reunions

Two major reunion councils attempted to heal the schism. The Council of Lyon (1274) produced a union that was politically motivated and rejected by Eastern clergy and laity almost immediately. The Council of Florence (1439) produced a more substantive agreement, signed under enormous pressure as the Ottoman Turks closed in on Constantinople. The Eastern delegates returned home to find their congregations unwilling to accept it. A famous line attributed to Byzantine Grand Duke Loukas Notaras captures the mood: "Better the Sultan's turban than the Cardinal's hat."

Constantinople fell to the Ottomans in 1453. The question of reunion was shelved by history.

Where Things Stand

In 1964, Pope Paul VI and Ecumenical Patriarch Athenagoras met in Jerusalem and "lifted" the mutual excommunications of 1054. The gesture was symbolic. It did not restore communion. But it signaled a new willingness to talk.

Since then, the Joint International Commission for Theological Dialogue between the Catholic Church and the Orthodox Church has been meeting regularly. Progress has been real but slow. The two sides have issued joint statements on several topics, including the sacraments and the nature of the Church. The hardest question — papal primacy — remains under discussion.

The Eastern Catholic Churches (Greek-Catholic, Melkite, Maronite, and others) exist as a kind of living bridge: communities that retain Eastern liturgy, theology, and spirituality while being in full communion with Rome. Their existence is both a sign of hope and a source of tension. For the Orthodox, they represent proselytism in Eastern dress. For the Eastern Catholics themselves, they represent a legitimate choice to maintain both their Eastern identity and their communion with the See of Peter.

An Irenic Conclusion

Assigning blame for the schism is easy. Too easy. Both sides contributed. Both sides have legitimate grievances and legitimate theological positions. The point is not to win the argument but to heal the wound.

The Eastern Christian who encounters the West with curiosity rather than defensiveness will find genuine holiness, serious theology, and a deep love of Christ. The Western Christian who encounters the East will find the same. The tragedy of the schism is not that one side was right and the other wrong. The tragedy is that the Body of Christ was torn, and the tearing diminished both halves.

Healing is not the same as pretending the differences don't exist. They do, and they matter. But the differences are best addressed within a relationship of mutual respect and shared prayer. The schism began with men walking away from each other. It can only end with men walking toward each other.

Further Reading

For a balanced overview, see Kallistos Ware's The Orthodox Church (chapters on the estrangement). Steven Runciman's The Eastern Schism remains the classic historical treatment. For the 1204 disaster, Jonathan Phillips's The Fourth Crusade and the Sack of Constantinople is gripping and well-documented. On the theological dialogue since Vatican II, see the joint statements published by the North American Orthodox-Catholic Theological Consultation.
