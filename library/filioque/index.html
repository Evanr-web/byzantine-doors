The Filioque Controversy

One word added to the Creed — 'and the Son' — and a theological fault line that has shaped Christianity for over a thousand years.

Category Theology
Read time 7 min read

The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, finalized at the Council of Constantinople in 381, says the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." Eight words in the Greek. Unambiguous. Conciliar. Settled.

Sometime in the sixth century, churches in Visigothic Spain began reciting something different: the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son. In Latin: filioque. One word. Three syllables. It spread through the Frankish kingdoms, reached Rome, and by 1014 was part of the papal liturgy.

The Eastern Churches have never accepted it. The disagreement is simultaneously about the inner life of the Trinity, the authority of ecumenical councils, and the nature of the Church itself. It is, pound for pound, the most consequential theological word in Christian history.

The Theological Question

The question sounds abstract: Does the Holy Spirit proceed from the Father alone, or from the Father and the Son together? To grasp why it matters, some trinitarian grammar is necessary.

In classical trinitarian theology, the three Persons of the Trinity are distinguished by their relations of origin. The Father is "unbegotten" — He is the source, the arche, of the divine life. The Son is "begotten" of the Father. The Spirit "proceeds" from the Father. These relations are what make the three Persons distinct. Remove the distinctions, and you collapse the Trinity into a single undifferentiated deity.

The Eastern position, following the Cappadocian Fathers: the Father alone is the monarchia — the single source or principle of the Trinity. The Son is begotten from the Father. The Spirit proceeds from the Father. Both Son and Spirit have their origin in the Father, and this shared origin is what guarantees the unity of God. The Father is the source. The Son and Spirit are the "two hands of God" (Irenaeus's image), equally divine, equally eternal, but both originating from the one Father.

The Western position, following Augustine: the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son as from a single principle. Augustine emphasized the relationship between Son and Spirit: the Spirit is the "bond of love" between Father and Son, the mutual love that the first two Persons share. In this framework, the filioque expresses the Spirit's relationship to the Son and prevents the Spirit from being disconnected from Christ.

The Father is Father, and is unoriginate, for He is of no one. The Son is Son, and is not unoriginate, for He is of the Father. The Spirit is truly Spirit, coming forth from the Father indeed, but not after the manner of the Son, for it is not by generation but by procession.
— Gregory of Nazianzus, Oration 31 (On the Holy Spirit), 4th century

Why the East Objects

The Eastern objections are both procedural and theological.

The Procedural Objection

The Creed was defined by an ecumenical council. Ecumenical councils represent the highest teaching authority in the undivided Church. No single patriarchate, including Rome, has the right to alter the Creed unilaterally. To do so is to claim an authority above the councils, which the East regards as a fundamental violation of the conciliar structure of the Church.

Even some Western theologians have acknowledged this point. The filioque was added without Eastern consultation, without conciliar approval, and without the consent of the churches that had composed and ratified the original Creed.

The Theological Objection

If the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, then the Father is no longer the sole source of the Trinity. The monarchia — the single-source principle that guarantees divine unity — is compromised. The East fears that the filioque introduces a subtle subordination of the Spirit to the Son, or alternatively, that it makes the Father less than fully the origin of divine life.

There is also a concern about trinitarian symmetry. In the Eastern model, the Father relates to Son and Spirit directly: He begets the one and spirates the other. In the filioque model, the Spirit's origin involves two Persons, creating an asymmetry that the East regards as theologically problematic.

Western Responses

Western theology has not been indifferent to these concerns. Several responses have emerged over the centuries.

Thomas Aquinas argued that the filioque is theologically necessary: if the Spirit does not proceed from the Son, there is no way to distinguish the Spirit from the Son. Both would simply "come from the Father," with no distinguishing relation between them.

More recently, the Catholic Church has shown significant flexibility. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (§248) acknowledges the legitimacy of the Eastern formula and describes the difference as complementary rather than contradictory. Pope John Paul II, in his 1995 encyclical Ut Unum Sint, signaled openness to discussing the filioque as part of ecumenical dialogue. When the Pope celebrates liturgy with Eastern Catholics, the Creed is recited without the filioque.

Some Western theologians have gone further, arguing that the filioque should be removed from the Creed — not because it is heretical, but because it was added improperly and remains a stumbling block to unity. The Anglican Lambeth Conference of 1978 recommended its removal. Several Reformed theologians have made similar arguments.

The original form of the Creed, without the Filioque, should be normative for all Christians.
— Joint Statement, North American Orthodox-Catholic Theological Consultation, 2003

The Deeper Issue

Behind the filioque controversy lies a larger question: How does the Church make doctrinal decisions? The East insists on conciliar consensus. The West (or at least the Catholic West) allows for papal authority to develop doctrine in ways that councils did not explicitly address.

The filioque is, in this sense, a test case for ecclesiology. It is not only about the Spirit's procession. It is about who has the right to define what Christians believe. Until that deeper question is addressed, the surface-level theological disagreement will remain unresolved.

An Irenic Note

Both traditions confess the same God, worship the same Trinity, and affirm the full divinity of the Holy Spirit. The disagreement is real, but it exists within a shared faith, not across a chasm of unbelief. Recent ecumenical dialogue has shown that the gap may be narrower than centuries of polemic have made it seem.

The filioque is worth understanding not as ammunition for argument but as an invitation to think more carefully about the Trinity — a mystery that no formula, Eastern or Western, can fully contain.

Further Reading

For a balanced and thorough treatment, see A. Edward Siecienski's The Filioque: History of a Doctrinal Controversy. Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church (chapter on the procession of the Spirit) provides the classic Eastern critique. For the Western perspective, Yves Congar's I Believe in the Holy Spirit is indispensable. The 2003 statement from the North American Orthodox-Catholic Theological Consultation, "The Filioque: A Church-Dividing Issue?", is available online and represents the best of recent ecumenical work on the topic.
