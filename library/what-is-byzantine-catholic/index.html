What Is a Byzantine Catholic?

Eastern tradition, Western communion — the Christians who hold both lungs together.

You walk into a Catholic church and the priest is married. The congregation is singing in a language you don't recognize. There is no organ, no pews, no statues — just voices rising together in ancient harmonies. Babies are receiving communion from a golden spoon. The walls are covered floor to ceiling in icons: Christ, the Theotokos, apostles and saints, all gazing back at you with enormous, quiet eyes. Incense fills the air so thickly you can taste it. This is Catholic — fully, completely, unambiguously Catholic — but it doesn't look like any Catholic church you have ever seen. Welcome to a Byzantine Catholic parish.

The Short Answer

A Byzantine Catholic is a member of one of several Eastern Catholic Churches that follow the Byzantine liturgical tradition while being in full communion with the Pope of Rome. They celebrate the Divine Liturgy instead of the Roman Mass. They baptize, chrismate, and commune infants all at once. Their priests can be married. Their churches are filled with icons instead of statues. Their theology breathes through different lungs than the Latin West — but it is the same faith, confessed since the apostles.

This is not a compromise between East and West. It is not a hybrid. It is not a Roman Catholic parish that borrowed some Eastern decorations. It is an ancient Christian tradition in its own right, older than the medieval papacy, older than the Roman Rite as we know it — a tradition that traces its roots to the apostolic churches of Constantinople, Antioch, and Jerusalem.

How Did This Happen?

The early Church was never liturgically uniform. From the very beginning, Christians in different regions worshipped differently. The Church in Rome developed the Roman Rite. The Church in Antioch developed the West Syrian and East Syrian traditions. The Church in Alexandria had its own way of praying. And the Church in Constantinople — the "New Rome," capital of the Eastern Roman Empire — developed what we now call the Byzantine Rite, drawing on the liturgical genius of saints like Basil the Great and John Chrysostom.

For the first thousand years, these diverse traditions existed within one Church. East and West were in communion. They argued — sometimes bitterly — about theology, jurisdiction, and authority. But they shared one faith and one eucharistic table.

Then came the Great Schism of 1054, and the slow, painful fracture between Rome and Constantinople. The division was never clean. It took centuries to harden. But by the late Middle Ages, the Christian East and the Christian West were functionally separated — two halves of what had been one Body.

Some Eastern Christians, however, chose to restore communion with Rome while keeping their Byzantine identity intact. The most significant of these reunions was the Union of Brest in 1596, when the majority of bishops in the Kyivan metropolitanate entered into full communion with the Pope. Similar unions happened elsewhere: the Melkites in the eighteenth century, the Romanians in Transylvania, the Slovaks, the Hungarians, and others at various points in history.

These communities are not converts from Orthodoxy. Many have been Catholic for four hundred years or more. The Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church — the largest Byzantine Catholic church — has maintained its communion with Rome through centuries of persecution, including the brutal suppression by the Soviet Union that drove it underground from 1946 to 1989. These are churches with deep roots, long memories, and a tested faith.

What Makes It "Byzantine"?

The word "Byzantine" refers to the liturgical tradition that developed in the city of Byzantium — later Constantinople, now Istanbul. When someone says "Byzantine Catholic," the "Byzantine" part describes how they worship. And the differences from a Roman Catholic parish are striking.

The Divine Liturgy. Byzantine Catholics celebrate the Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom (or, on certain occasions, the Divine Liturgy of St. Basil the Great). The structure, prayers, and rhythm are entirely different from the Roman Mass. The liturgy is almost entirely sung. There are no "low" liturgies — even a weekday service involves chanting.

Icons everywhere. The church is filled with icons — sacred images painted according to a theological tradition, not merely an artistic one. The most prominent feature is the iconostasis, the icon screen that separates the nave from the sanctuary. It is not a wall to hide things. It is a wall to reveal them — a visual theology of heaven meeting earth.

Full initiation at baptism. In the Byzantine tradition, baptism, chrismation (confirmation), and first communion are administered together — even to infants. A baby who is baptized on Sunday receives the Eucharist that same day. There is no waiting until second grade. The logic is simple: if baptism truly makes you a member of Christ's Body, why would you withhold the Body's food?

Communion by spoon. The consecrated bread is placed into the chalice of consecrated wine, and both species are administered together on a golden spoon. Everyone — from the oldest parishioner to the youngest infant — receives communion the same way.

Married clergy. Byzantine Catholic priests may be married — provided they marry before ordination to the diaconate. This is not a modern innovation. It is the ancient practice of the Eastern Church, maintained continuously for two thousand years. Bishops, however, are chosen from among celibate clergy (typically monks).

Byzantine chant. The music is unaccompanied. No organ, no piano, no guitar. Just human voices. The chant traditions vary by community — Ukrainian, Ruthenian, Melkite, Romanian — but the principle is the same: the human voice is the proper instrument of worship.

A different calendar. Some Byzantine Catholic communities follow the Julian calendar (which currently runs thirteen days behind the Gregorian), while others have adopted the Revised Julian calendar. This means Christmas might be on December 25 or January 7, depending on the parish.

Serious fasting. The Byzantine fasting discipline is stricter than the Latin one. The Great Fast (Lent) means no meat, no dairy, no eggs, no fish, no wine, no oil for forty days. There are also fasting periods before Christmas (the Phillip's Fast), before the feast of Saints Peter and Paul, and before the Dormition of the Theotokos in August. In practice, individual observance varies widely — but the discipline itself has never been relaxed.

What Makes It "Catholic"?

The "Catholic" part means exactly what you think it means: full communion with the Bishop of Rome. A Byzantine Catholic and a Roman Catholic share one faith, one communion, and one Church.

Full communion with the Pope. Byzantine Catholics recognize the primacy of the Pope of Rome. Their bishops are in communion with him. Their churches are part of the universal Catholic Church.

All seven sacraments, fully valid. Baptism, chrismation, Eucharist, confession, anointing of the sick, marriage, holy orders — all celebrated, all recognized, all valid. A Catholic of any rite can walk into a Byzantine Catholic parish and receive communion. And a Byzantine Catholic can walk into a Roman Catholic parish and do the same. There is no re-initiation, no transfer required. You are Catholic. Period.

Shared doctrine on the essentials. The Trinity. The Incarnation. The Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. The communion of saints. The resurrection of the body. The fundamental dogmas of the faith are held in common across all Catholic rites.

Their own canon law. Eastern Catholic churches are governed by the Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches (CCEO), not the Code of Canon Law (CIC) that governs the Latin Church. This is not a concession. It is a recognition that the Eastern churches have their own ancient legal and disciplinary traditions.

Since, in fact, we believe that the venerable and ancient tradition of the Eastern Churches is an integral part of the heritage of Christ's Church, the first need for Catholics is to be familiar with that tradition, so as to be nourished by it.
— Pope John Paul II, Orientale Lumen §1 (1995)

How Is It Different from Orthodox?

This is the question everyone asks. The honest answer is: if you walked into a Byzantine Catholic church and an Orthodox church on the same Sunday, you might not be able to tell the difference from the pew.

Liturgically: almost identical. The same Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom. The same iconostasis. The same chant. The same vestments. The same incense. A visitor who wandered in off the street would not be able to say which was which.

Theologically: very close, with some real distinctions. The Filioque — whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from the Father "and the Son" — is the most famous point of historical disagreement between East and West. Byzantine Catholics, like the Orthodox, do not include the Filioque in the Creed; this is a Latin addition, not a Byzantine one. The real ecclesial difference is communion: Byzantine Catholics accept the primacy of the Pope of Rome and pray for him in the liturgy. The Orthodox do not. The Immaculate Conception is understood differently in Eastern and Western theological frameworks. But on most of what matters — Christology, Trinitarian theology, sacramental life, eschatology — the two traditions are remarkably close.

Ecclesially: this is where the real difference lives. Byzantine Catholics are in communion with Rome. The Orthodox are not. That single fact — communion or its absence — is the fundamental dividing line. Everything else is secondary.

Culturally: it varies enormously. A Melkite parish in Brooklyn will feel different from a Ukrainian Greek-Catholic parish in Lviv, which will feel different from a Ruthenian parish in Pittsburgh. The Byzantine tradition is not monolithic. It is a family of churches, each with its own history, language, music, and customs.

The Catholic Church holds in high esteem the institutions, liturgical rites, ecclesiastical traditions, and the established standards of the Christian life of the Eastern Churches.
— Second Vatican Council, Orientalium Ecclesiarum §1 (1964)

The Major Byzantine Catholic Churches

There is no single "Byzantine Catholic Church." There are several, each with its own hierarchy, history, and character:

  • Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church — the largest, with roughly five million faithful. Centered in Ukraine, with a significant diaspora in Canada, the United States, Brazil, and elsewhere. Led by a Major Archbishop based in Kyiv.
  • Melkite Greek-Catholic Church — rooted in the ancient patriarchates of Antioch, Alexandria, and Jerusalem. Centered in Syria, Lebanon, and the broader Middle East, with a growing diaspora.
  • Ruthenian Greek-Catholic Church — also called the Byzantine Catholic Church in America. Primarily in the United States, with roots in the Carpathian region of Central Europe.
  • Romanian Greek-Catholic Church — in Transylvania and beyond. Suppressed by the communist regime in 1948 and revived after 1989.
  • Slovak Greek-Catholic Church, Hungarian Greek-Catholic Church — smaller churches with deep local roots in Central Europe.

A note on terminology: "Greek Catholic" does not mean Greek. It means "following the Greek (Byzantine) liturgical tradition." A Ukrainian Greek-Catholic is Ukrainian, not Greek. The name is historical, reflecting the Byzantine Rite's origins in the Greek-speaking world.

Common Misconceptions

"So you're Orthodox?" — No. Byzantine Catholics are in full communion with the Bishop of Rome. Liturgically similar, ecclesially distinct.

"So you're Roman Catholics who like Eastern stuff?" — No. The Byzantine Catholic tradition is not a Latin parish with borrowed aesthetics. It is an independent, ancient tradition with its own theology, spirituality, canon law, and liturgical life. It has been Eastern for as long as there has been an East.

"Did you convert from Orthodoxy?" — Most Byzantine Catholic communities have been Catholic for centuries. The Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church has been in communion with Rome since 1596. The Melkites since 1724. These are not recent converts. They are ancient churches.

"Can you go to a regular Catholic church?" — Yes. And a Roman Catholic can attend a Byzantine Catholic liturgy and receive communion there. All Catholics share one communion, regardless of rite. You do not need permission, a transfer, or a special dispensation.

"Is the Pope your boss?" — Byzantine Catholics recognize papal primacy — the Pope as the visible head of the universal Church. But Byzantine Catholic churches have their own hierarchs (patriarchs, major archbishops, metropolitans), their own canon law (CCEO), and significant liturgical and disciplinary autonomy. The relationship is real communion, not micromanagement.

LAYER 2 Christ Our Pascha on Eastern Catholic Identity
▾

Christ Our Pascha is the catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church, published in 2011. It is itself an act of identity — the most comprehensive self-expression of what it means to be Byzantine Catholic in the modern world. The catechism opens (§§ 1–10) by situating the UGCC within the broader Christian tradition: rooted in the apostolic faith of the undivided Church, heir to the Byzantine theological and liturgical patrimony, and in full communion with the See of Rome.

If you want to know what Byzantine Catholics believe, Christ Our Pascha is not just a reference — it is the answer. It presents the faith not as a set of doctrinal propositions borrowed from either East or West, but as a living tradition received from the apostles, articulated by the Fathers, celebrated in the liturgy, and lived in the daily practice of a particular people. Its very existence is an argument: that being Eastern and being Catholic are not competing identities but complementary ones, two expressions of one apostolic faith.

Experience It

If you want to know what a Byzantine Catholic is, the best answer is not on this page. It is in a church. Find a Byzantine Catholic parish near you. Attend a Sunday Divine Liturgy. You will understand more in one hour of worship than in any amount of reading. Come early. Stand if others stand. Follow the incense with your eyes. Let the chant wash over you. You will know.
