Glossary

Words You'll Encounter

Eastern Christianity has its own vocabulary. Here's every word explained in plain language.

A
Akathist
/ah-KAH-thist/
A hymn of praise sung while standing (the word literally means "not sitting"). Usually dedicated to Christ, the Theotokos, or a saint. One of the most beloved forms of Eastern prayer.
Ancestral Sin
/an-SES-tral sin/
The Eastern understanding of the Fall. Rather than inheriting Adam's guilt (the Western "original sin" concept), humanity inherits the consequences: mortality, weakness, and a tendency toward sin.
Antidoron
/an-TID-oh-ron/
Blessed bread distributed after Liturgy to all present — not the Eucharist, but a sign of fellowship. The word means "instead of the gift." Anyone may receive it.
Antimension
/an-tee-MEN-see-on/
A cloth containing relics of saints, placed on the altar and signed by the bishop. The Divine Liturgy cannot be served without it — it is the bishop's authorization made tangible.
Axios
/AHK-see-os/
"He is worthy!" — exclaimed by the congregation at an ordination, affirming the community's acceptance of the newly ordained deacon, priest, or bishop.
C
Chrismation
/kriz-MAY-shun/
Anointing with holy chrism oil, the Eastern equivalent of Confirmation. Received immediately after Baptism — even for infants. Seals the gift of the Holy Spirit.
D
Deacon
/DEE-kun/
An ordained minister who assists at Liturgy — proclaiming the Gospel, leading the litanies, censing the church. The word means "servant."
Divine Liturgy
/dih-VINE LIT-er-jee/
The central worship service of the Eastern Church. Almost entirely sung, rich in ritual, and understood as a genuine participation in the worship of heaven.
Dormition
/dor-MIH-shun/
The "falling asleep" of the Theotokos (the Mother of God). Not "death" but a peaceful passing into eternal life. A major feast celebrated August 15.
E
Ectenia
/ek-teh-NEE-ah/
A litany of petitions led by the deacon, to which the congregation responds "Lord, have mercy" or "Grant this, O Lord." The structural backbone of the Liturgy's prayers.
Epiklesis
/eh-PIK-leh-sis/
The prayer invoking the Holy Spirit to transform bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ. The East emphasizes this moment; the West historically emphasized the "words of institution."
Epitaphios
/eh-pih-TAH-fee-os/
An embroidered cloth depicting Christ's burial, placed on a bier and venerated on Holy Friday. Carried in procession around the church in one of the most solemn moments of the liturgical year.
G
Great Fast
/grayt fast/
The 40-day Lenten period before Pascha (Easter). The strictest fasting season in the Eastern calendar — no meat, dairy, fish, wine, or oil on most days.
H
Hesychasm
/HEZ-ih-kazm/
A contemplative prayer tradition emphasizing inner stillness (hesychia) and the repetition of the Jesus Prayer. Associated with Mount Athos and St. Gregory Palamas.
Holy Unction
/HOH-lee UNK-shun/
The sacrament of anointing the sick with blessed oil, accompanied by prayers for healing of body and soul. Served by multiple priests when possible.
Horologion
/hor-oh-LOH-gee-on/
The "Book of Hours" — the liturgical book containing the fixed prayers for each hour of the day. The backbone of the Eastern prayer cycle.
I
Iconostasis
/eye-koh-NAH-stah-sis/
The wall of icons between the nave and the sanctuary. It has three doors: the Royal Doors in the center, and two smaller doors on the sides. Every icon has a specific place and meaning.
K
Kontakion
/kon-TAH-kee-on/
A short hymn summarizing the theological meaning of a feast day, paired with the troparion. Together they form the musical "thesis and response" of each celebration.
L
Litiya
/lih-TEE-yah/
A special blessing of bread, wheat, wine, and oil performed at Vespers on the eve of major feasts. The blessed bread is distributed to the faithful afterward.
M
Menaion
/meh-NAY-on/
A liturgical book organized by month, containing the hymns, readings, and prayers for each saint's commemoration and feast day. There are twelve volumes — one for each month.
O
Octoechos
/ok-TOH-eh-hos/
The system of eight musical tones that cycle weekly through the liturgical year. Each tone has its own melodies and hymn texts, giving each week a distinct musical character.
P
Pantocrator
/pan-TOH-krah-tor/
"Ruler of All" — the title and iconic depiction of Christ, usually painted in the central dome of a church. He gazes down with an expression that is both fierce and tender, holding a Gospel book.
Pascha
/PAH-skah/
Easter — the Feast of Feasts, the Resurrection of Christ. The most important celebration in the Eastern calendar. "Christ is risen!" is the greeting for forty days.
Prokeimenon
/pro-KAY-meh-non/
A psalm verse chanted before Scripture readings in the Liturgy, setting the tone for the passage that follows. The word means "that which is set before."
R
Royal Doors
/ROY-al dorz/
The central doors of the iconostasis, through which only clergy may pass. They open at key moments in the Liturgy — when Christ, the King of Glory, comes to the people.
T
Theosis
/thee-OH-sis/
Divinization — the process of becoming united with God by grace. The ultimate purpose of human existence in Eastern theology. "God became human so that humans might become God" (St. Athanasius).
Theotokos
/thee-oh-TOH-kos/
Literally "God-bearer" — the title given to Mary at the Council of Ephesus (431 AD). It's a statement about Jesus — that the one she bore was truly God.
Trisagion
/tri-SAH-gee-on/
"Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us." One of the most ancient hymns in Christianity, sung at every Divine Liturgy and in daily prayers.
Troparion
/troh-PAR-ee-on/
A short hymn that captures the theme of a feast day, saint, or liturgical occasion. Think of it as the "thesis statement" of the day in musical form.
Byzantine Doors
Catechetical and parish resources for the Byzantine Christian tradition
Byzantine Doors is an independent educational project created by a layperson to help English-speaking seekers and families discover the Byzantine Christian tradition. Theological content draws primarily from Christ Our Pascha, the catechism of the Ukrainian Greek-Catholic Church. English hymn texts are sourced from publicly available translations and may differ from those used in your parish. This site is not an official publication of any eparchy, diocese, or church body. For authoritative guidance on liturgical practice, fasting, and prayer, please consult your pastor.
