Why Does Beauty Matter?

Why do humans build cathedrals instead of warehouses? What if beauty is not decoration — but a signal?

Home › Narthex › Why Does Beauty Matter?
Section The Narthex
Read time 9 min read

There is a moment, if you are paying attention, when a sunset stops you. Not because you decided to appreciate it. Not because you made an aesthetic judgment. It happens before thought. Your body goes still, your breath catches, and for a fraction of a second, you are simply there — completely present, completely absorbed. Then the moment passes, and you go back to your phone.

What was that?

We have perfectly good evolutionary explanations for why food tastes good and sex feels pleasant. Survival and reproduction. But what survival advantage does a sunset confer? Why do humans stand in front of paintings and weep? Why did our ancestors, living at subsistence level, spend decades building structures of staggering complexity and covering them in gold — not for shelter, not for defense, but for something they could only call glory?

The modern answer, broadly speaking, is that beauty is subjective. A preference. A neural firing pattern shaped by culture and conditioning. "Beauty is in the eye of the beholder" — the cliché so familiar it feels like a law of physics. And perhaps that settles it. Perhaps a cathedral is just an expensive warehouse with better lighting.

But there is an older answer. And it is far more interesting.

The Cathedral Problem

In 532 AD, the Emperor Justinian commissioned a building in Constantinople. It took five years and ten thousand workers. The interior was sheathed in gold mosaic, marble quarried from across the known world, and windows positioned so that light itself became a building material — shifting, moving, transforming the space throughout the day. When Justinian entered the completed Hagia Sophia for the first time, he is said to have whispered: "Solomon, I have surpassed you."

The building was a church. But calling it a church is like calling the ocean "some water." Hagia Sophia was a theological argument made in stone and light. Every proportion, every surface, every relationship between space and brightness was designed to communicate something specific: that the material world is not all there is. That matter can become a window.

Justinian was not an eccentric. The Eastern Roman Empire spent extraordinary resources on beauty — not as luxury, but as theology. Icons, mosaics, liturgical chant, the design of sacred space: all of it was understood as a form of revelation. Beauty was not decoration applied to faith. Beauty was how faith became visible.

This was not unique to Christians. The ancient Greeks had a word — kalos — that meant both "beautiful" and "good." For Plato, beauty was not a feeling. It was a property of reality, as real as weight or temperature. Beautiful things participated in the Form of Beauty itself. To encounter beauty was to encounter something true about the structure of existence.

The question is whether the Greeks and the cathedral builders were deluded, or whether we are the ones who lost something.

The Via Pulchritudinis

There is a Latin phrase that keeps surfacing in philosophical discussion: via pulchritudinis — the way of beauty. It names the idea that beauty is not just pleasant but epistemic: a way of knowing. That the experience of beauty gives us access to truths that logic and measurement cannot reach on their own.

Beauty will save the world.
— Fyodor Dostoevsky, The Idiot

Dostoevsky's famous line is usually quoted out of context, turned into a bumper sticker. But the character who says it, Prince Myshkin, is not making a sentimental claim. He is making an almost desperate one. In a world where rational argument has failed to prevent cruelty, where ideology produces atrocity, where words have been corrupted past meaning — beauty remains uncorrupted. You cannot argue with it. You cannot deconstruct it. It simply is, and its presence changes you before you have time to resist.

This is the scandalous claim at the heart of the via pulchritudinis: that beauty is a form of evidence. Not proof, not argument, but evidence — the kind that works on the whole person, not just the intellect.

Alexander Schmemann, a twentieth-century theologian, put it this way: the liturgy is "not a sermon illustrated by beautiful ceremonies, but a beautiful ceremony that is itself the sermon." The medium is not decorating the message. The medium is the message. The beauty of the chant, the incense, the candlelight, the gold — these are not there to make worship more palatable. They are the worship. They are communicating something that cannot be said in words.

LAYER 2 The theology behind beauty as revelation
▾

In the Eastern Christian tradition, the material world is understood as fundamentally sacramental — capable of bearing and communicating divine presence. This is not pantheism (God is the world) but what theologians call panentheism in its orthodox sense: God is present in and through the material world without being reduced to it.

The theological foundation is the Incarnation: if God became matter (took on flesh, breathed air, ate bread), then matter is capable of carrying the divine. This is why Eastern Christianity rejected iconoclasm — the destruction of religious images — at the Seventh Ecumenical Council in 787. If Christ took on a human face, then that face can be depicted. And if it can be depicted, then an image can become a meeting point between the human and the divine.

Beauty, in this framework, is not an accident. It is a property of reality as God made it. When something is beautiful, it is functioning correctly — revealing the divine intention embedded in its nature. Ugliness, then, is not just displeasing. It is a sign of distortion, of something pulled away from its purpose.

The Icon Tradition

If you walk into an Eastern Christian church, you will be surrounded by faces. Icons — painted on wood, set in mosaic, woven into fabric — cover the walls, the ceiling, the screen that separates the altar from the congregation. They are everywhere. And they are watching you.

To a modern Western eye, icons look strange. The proportions are wrong. The perspective is reversed. Faces are elongated, eyes are enormous, backgrounds are gold rather than naturalistic. If you judge them by Renaissance standards — accurate anatomy, linear perspective, emotional realism — they fail.

But they are not trying to do what Renaissance painting does. They are trying to do something completely different.

An icon is not a picture of a holy person. It is understood as a window. The gold background is not decorative — it represents uncreated light, the light that has no source in this world. The reversed perspective means the vanishing point is not behind the image (as in Western painting) but in front of it — in you. The icon is not a scene you look into. It is a presence that looks out at you.

This is why Orthodox Christians do not say they "look at" an icon. They say they "venerate" it — which literally means they honor it with a kiss. Not because the wood and paint are holy, but because beauty, rightly made and rightly received, opens a door.

The icon tradition encodes a very specific claim about beauty: that it is relational. Beauty is not a property an object has in isolation. It is what happens between the object and the person who encounters it. A sunset is beautiful because it does something to you — it creates an opening, a receptivity, a momentary dropping of the defenses that normally keep you locked inside your own head.

What We Lost

Something happened in Western culture between the Enlightenment and now. Beauty went from being a fundamental category of reality — alongside truth and goodness — to being a matter of personal taste. Aesthetics became a subdiscipline of philosophy, then a branch of psychology, then a marketing strategy. "Beauty" became a word for selling cosmetics.

The consequences are not just philosophical. They are visible in the built environment. Compare any medieval city center — Bruges, Siena, Prague — with the average North American suburb built after 1950. The older cities are built as if beauty matters, as if the spaces humans inhabit should nourish the soul. The newer ones are built as if efficiency is the only value and the human eye has no needs.

Roger Scruton, the philosopher, argued that the disappearance of beauty from architecture was not just an aesthetic loss but a spiritual one. When we build ugly buildings, ugly cities, ugly schools, we are making a statement about human dignity — whether we intend to or not. We are saying that what the eye sees and the heart feels does not matter. That function is enough. That the soul is not real.

So perhaps that old trinity of Truth, Goodness, and Beauty is not simply the dressed-up, worn-out formula we thought it was. If the tops of these three trees do converge, as thinkers have always maintained, and if the too-obvious, too-straight sprouts of Truth and Goodness have been knocked down, cut off — then perhaps the whimsical, unpredictable, unexpected branches of Beauty will work their way through and soar up to that very place, and thus complete the work of all three.
— Alexander Solzhenitsyn, Nobel Lecture, 1970

Beauty as Resistance

There is a story from the eleventh century, recorded in the Primary Chronicle of Kievan Rus'. Prince Vladimir of Kyiv sent envoys to investigate the world's major religions before choosing one for his people. They visited the Muslims in Bulgaria, the Catholics in Germany, and the Eastern Christians in Constantinople. Of the first two, the report was lukewarm. But of Constantinople, the envoys said this:

"We knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth. For on earth there is no such splendor or beauty, and we are at a loss how to describe it. We only know that God dwells there among the people, and their service is fairer than the ceremonies of other nations."

Set aside the historical accuracy (the story is probably embellished). What matters is what it reveals about a culture's priorities. The envoys did not report on the logical coherence of the theology. They did not evaluate the ethical system. They were overwhelmed by beauty — and in that overwhelm, they encountered something they could only describe as the presence of God.

This is the ancient claim, stripped bare: beauty is not a nice extra. It is a primary mode of encounter with what is real. It works on parts of the human person that argument cannot reach. It bypasses the intellect's defenses and speaks directly to something deeper — call it the soul, the heart, the core of awareness. And what it says, over and over, in sunsets and in cathedrals and in the faces of icons, is: there is more.

LAYER 2 Why the Eastern tradition resisted iconoclasm
▾

In the eighth and ninth centuries, the Byzantine Empire was torn apart by the Iconoclast Controversy — the question of whether religious images should be permitted or destroyed. The iconoclasts (image-breakers) argued that images of Christ and the saints were idolatrous. The iconodules (image-defenders) argued that to deny the possibility of depicting Christ was to deny the reality of the Incarnation itself.

The argument the iconodules won — at the Seventh Ecumenical Council in 787 — was not simply about art. It was about the nature of matter. If God became material, then material things can bear the divine. Destroying icons was not just vandalism. It was a theological statement that matter is unredeemable, that the physical world cannot carry spiritual meaning.

The defense of icons was, at its root, a defense of beauty's theological legitimacy. The Eastern tradition said: beauty is not a distraction from God. It is one of the primary ways God makes Himself known.

The Signal

Here is the thing about beauty that the modern "it's subjective" framework cannot explain: beauty makes demands. A beautiful landscape does not just please you. It calls you to attention. A great piece of music does not just entertain you. It reorganizes you. You come out of the experience different than you went in — more open, more aware, more alive to the textures of existence.

If beauty is merely subjective — just neurons firing, just conditioning, just preference — then these experiences are illusions. The sunset is not really telling you anything. The cathedral is just a big room. The icon is painted wood and nothing more.

But if beauty is a signal — if it is, as the old traditions claimed, a real property of reality pointing toward something beyond itself — then the experience of beauty is not an illusion but a perception. You are perceiving something real. Something that your measuring instruments cannot detect but that your consciousness registers with absolute clarity.

There is an ancient tradition, largely forgotten in the modern West but still very much alive in the East, that took this possibility seriously. That built its worship, its theology, its entire civilization around the conviction that beauty is not decoration but revelation. That a sunset and a cathedral and an icon are all doing the same thing: opening a door.

Whether you walk through it is, of course, up to you.
