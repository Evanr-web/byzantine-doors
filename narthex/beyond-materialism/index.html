Is There More to Reality Than What We Can Measure?

Science tells us what. It doesn't tell us why. There's a tradition that never confused the two.

Home › Narthex › Is There More to Reality Than What We Can Measure?
Section The Narthex
Read time 10 min read

Here is a question that science, by its own design, cannot answer: why is there something rather than nothing?

This is not a failure of science. It is a feature. Science is a method for investigating how the natural world behaves — what happens, under what conditions, in what patterns. It is breathtakingly good at this. It has given us antibiotics, satellites, and an understanding of the universe's age accurate to within a few percent. But it operates by deliberately limiting its scope. It studies what can be measured, tested, and repeated. Questions that fall outside that scope — questions of meaning, purpose, consciousness, why the laws of physics are what they are rather than something else — are not scientific questions. They are philosophical ones.

The modern West has largely forgotten this distinction. We have quietly adopted a metaphysical position — materialism, the belief that physical matter is all that exists — and mistaken it for a conclusion of science. It is not. It is a philosophical assumption that science neither proves nor requires. Many of the greatest scientists in history — Newton, Faraday, Heisenberg, Planck — were not materialists. They simply did not confuse their method with a worldview.

There is a tradition, largely unknown in the English-speaking world, that never made this confusion. That developed a sophisticated philosophical framework for thinking about what lies beyond measurement — not by rejecting reason, but by recognizing its limits. That tradition has something to say to anyone who has ever suspected that the material account of reality, however accurate, is not the whole story.

The Science-Religion War That Wasn't

The "conflict between science and religion" is one of the great myths of modern intellectual history. It is largely a product of two nineteenth-century books — John William Draper's History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom (1896) — both of which have been thoroughly debunked by historians of science. The actual history is far more interesting.

The medieval university system, where modern science was incubated, was a creation of the Church. The scientific method itself owes significant debts to theological assumptions: that the universe is rational (because it was created by a rational God), that it operates according to consistent laws (because God is faithful), and that human reason is capable of understanding those laws (because humans are made in the image of a rational Creator). These are not scientific conclusions. They are metaphysical premises without which science as we know it would never have gotten off the ground.

But there is a more specific point. The "science vs. religion" narrative is overwhelmingly a Western story — a product of specific developments in Latin Christianity: the Galileo affair, the Protestant fundamentalist reaction to Darwin, the American creation-evolution debate. The Eastern Christian tradition was never a party to any of it.

Do not suppose that to explain creation I am going to bring forth for you the inventions of my own mind. I will cite the words of the Holy Spirit, adding nothing of my own to the divine words. But I do not ask you to ignore the knowledge of the natural world.
— St. Basil the Great, Hexaemeron, 4th century

Basil of Caesarea, writing in the fourth century, composed a series of homilies on the creation account in Genesis. He drew freely on the natural philosophy of his day — Aristotelian physics, Hippocratic medicine, astronomical observation. He did not see any conflict between the two. Scripture tells us who created and why. Natural investigation tells us how. They are answering different questions about the same reality.

This approach — not harmonization, not conflict, but complementarity — has been the default Eastern position for seventeen centuries. There was no Eastern Galileo affair because the Eastern tradition never claimed that Scripture was a science textbook. There was no Eastern fundamentalist movement because the Eastern tradition never reduced truth to literal-historical factuality.

The Church Fathers recognized multiple levels of meaning in Scripture. Origen of Alexandria (3rd century) described three layers: the literal (or historical), the moral (what it teaches about how to live), and the spiritual (what it reveals about God and the soul's journey toward Him). Other Fathers used different schemes, but the principle was the same: the literal meaning is real but not always the primary meaning.

This means that a text like the Genesis creation account can be read as theologically true — God is the source of all existence, creation is good, humanity bears a special dignity — without requiring it to be a scientific description of physical processes. The Fathers were not naive. They knew the text was not a physics lecture. They were interested in what it revealed about God, about human nature, and about the relationship between the two.

This is not "liberal" biblical interpretation. It is the original method, practiced by the Church from the beginning, long before the modern concepts of "liberal" and "conservative" existed.

The Apophatic Tradition

If the modern West has a default mode for thinking about God, it is kataphatic — positive, descriptive. God is good. God is powerful. God is loving. God is a father. God is a king. We pile up descriptions, images, concepts, and try to build a mental model.

The Eastern tradition does this too. But it insists, with extraordinary rigor, that every positive statement about God must be accompanied by its own negation. God is good — but not "good" in any sense we can fully comprehend. God is powerful — but His power is nothing like what we mean by power. God exists — but not in the way that anything else exists.

This is apophatic theology: the way of negation. Knowing God by acknowledging what God is not. It sounds paradoxical. It is. That is the point.

The key figure here is a mysterious sixth-century writer known as Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite. His treatise The Mystical Theology is barely ten pages long and is one of the most influential texts in the history of Christian thought. His argument: God is so far beyond anything the human mind can conceive that our most accurate theological statements are negative ones. God is not a being among beings. God is not a thing. God does not "exist" in the way that tables and galaxies exist. God is beyond being itself.

It is beyond assertion and denial. We make assertions and denials of what is next to it, but never of it, for it is both beyond every assertion, being the perfect and unique cause of all things, and, by virtue of its preeminently simple and absolute nature, free of every limitation, beyond every denial.
— Pseudo-Dionysius, The Mystical Theology, c. 500 AD

This is not agnosticism. Dionysius is not saying we cannot know God. He is saying that God is known through a different kind of knowledge — not intellectual comprehension but direct encounter. The mind reaches its limit, falls silent, and in that silence, something else becomes possible. Not ignorance. Unknowing — a knowing beyond knowing.

If this sounds like mystical hand-waving, consider: modern physics has arrived at strikingly similar territory. Quantum mechanics tells us that reality at its most fundamental level does not behave like anything we can picture. Wave-particle duality, quantum entanglement, the measurement problem — these are not merely unsolved puzzles. They are indications that the deepest layer of physical reality exceeds our conceptual apparatus. The universe is, at bottom, weirder than we can think.

The apophatic tradition would not be surprised.

Essence and Energies

In the fourteenth century, a monk named Gregory Palamas, living on Mount Athos in Greece, was confronted with a challenge. A philosopher from Calabria named Barlaam argued that the monks who claimed to experience the presence of God directly — through intense prayer — were deluded. God is so transcendent, Barlaam said, that direct experience is impossible. All we can know of God is what we learn through philosophical reasoning.

Palamas disagreed. And his response produced one of the most important philosophical distinctions in the history of theology.

Palamas distinguished between God's essence and God's energies. God's essence — what God is in Himself — is indeed beyond all knowledge, experience, and participation. No creature can know it. Barlaam was right about that. But God's energies — His grace, His light, His love, His creative power — are genuinely God, not created intermediaries, and they can be experienced. When the monks experienced the divine light in prayer, they were not hallucinating. They were encountering God's real presence — God Himself acting and communicating, without the unknowable essence being breached.

▾

The centerpiece of Palamas's argument was the Transfiguration of Christ on Mount Tabor (Matthew 17:1-8). When Jesus was transfigured before Peter, James, and John, His face shone like the sun and His garments became white as light. Palamas argued that this light was not created — not a visual effect, not reflected sunlight, not a metaphor. It was the uncreated energy of God, shining through Christ's human nature.

The same light, Palamas taught, is available to all human beings through prayer and the sacraments. The saints who are described as "glowing" or "radiating light" in hagiographical literature are not being described in metaphorical terms. They are experiencing the same uncreated light that shone on Tabor.

The essence-energies distinction allows Eastern theology to hold two truths simultaneously: God is absolutely transcendent (in His essence) and absolutely present (in His energies). This avoids both the Western tendency toward a purely philosophical God (known only through reason) and the pantheistic tendency to collapse God into the world.

The essence-energies distinction solves a problem that has haunted Western philosophy of religion for centuries. Western theology, influenced by Thomas Aquinas, tended toward a God known primarily through rational argument — the Unmoved Mover, the First Cause, the God of the philosophers. When the Enlightenment showed that these arguments were less airtight than they appeared, the Western God became vulnerable. If God is primarily a conclusion of an argument, then a better argument can undo Him.

The Eastern God was never primarily a conclusion of an argument. He was primarily an encounter. The arguments matter — Eastern theologians are rigorous thinkers — but they are not the foundation. The foundation is the experience of divine presence, testified to by centuries of monks, mystics, and ordinary believers who practiced a specific discipline of prayer and reported consistent results.

A Different Axis

The modern debate about God tends to operate on a single axis: believe or don't believe. Accept the propositions or reject them. The God hypothesis versus the naturalist hypothesis.

The Eastern tradition operates on a different axis entirely. The question is not "Does God exist?" — a question that reduces God to an object whose existence can be affirmed or denied like the existence of dark matter. The question is: Is reality exhausted by what can be measured?

If the answer is yes — if measurement is the only valid form of knowledge — then we live in a universe where consciousness is an epiphenomenon, beauty is a neural accident, love is a biochemical strategy, and the fact that anything exists at all is a brute fact with no explanation. This is a coherent position. Many intelligent people hold it.

But if the answer is no — if reality has dimensions that exceed what instruments can detect — then we need a framework for thinking about those dimensions. Not a flight from reason, but an extension of it. A way of knowing that includes the empirical but does not stop there.

The Eastern Christian tradition offers such a framework. It is not the only one. But it has several distinctive features worth noting.

It does not ask you to believe things that contradict observed reality. It does not require a young Earth, or a denial of evolution, or a rejection of any established scientific finding. It operates in a domain that science, by design, does not enter.

It does not ask you to take claims on blind faith. It offers a practice — prayer, fasting, communal worship, self-examination — and says: do this consistently, over time, and report what you find. The Eastern tradition is, in this sense, almost empirical. It makes claims about the effects of certain practices on human consciousness and invites you to test them.

It takes mystery seriously without abandoning reason. The apophatic tradition is not anti-intellectual. It is what happens when the intellect follows its own logic to the limit and discovers that the limit is real. What lies beyond is not nonsense. It is a different kind of sense — one that requires a different kind of attention.

The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know.
— Blaise Pascal, Pensées, 1670

Pascal was a mathematician. He was not anti-reason. He was pointing to something the Eastern tradition had known for over a millennium: that the human person has capacities for knowledge that exceed the strictly rational. That the deepest truths are not discovered by argument alone but by a kind of attentive openness — what the Eastern Fathers called the nous, the spiritual intellect, the eye of the heart.

Whether this is true — whether there is a mode of knowing that operates beyond measurement and logic — is not something that can be settled by an article. It can only be settled by attention. By paying careful, sustained, honest attention to the full range of human experience and asking: is the materialist account sufficient? Does it explain everything you know yourself to have experienced?

If it does, then materialism is your answer, and it is an honest one.

If it doesn't — if there are dimensions of your experience that the measuring instruments cannot reach — then you might find it worth knowing that there is a tradition, very old and very much alive, that has been thinking about exactly this for a very long time.
