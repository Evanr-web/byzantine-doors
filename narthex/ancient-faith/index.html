What Did Ancient Christians Actually Believe?

Before the denominations, before the culture wars, before the splits — what did the first thousand years look like?

Home › Narthex › What Did Ancient Christians Actually Believe?
Section The Narthex
Read time 10 min read

Most people in the English-speaking world, if they think about Christianity at all, think about the version they grew up with — or the version they rejected. Evangelical megachurches. Catholic guilt. Televangelists. The culture wars. "Are you saved?" The prosperity gospel. Altar calls and hellfire sermons and arguments about whether the Earth is six thousand years old.

Here is a fact that, once you hear it, is hard to unhear: none of that existed for the first thousand years of Christianity. Not any of it. Not even close.

For roughly a millennium — from the apostles to the Great Schism of 1054 — there was, broadly speaking, one Church. It argued with itself constantly (the arguments were spectacular), but it held together. It produced a body of theology, a form of worship, and a way of understanding human existence that is almost unrecognizable compared to what most modern Westerners call "Christianity."

That tradition did not disappear. It is still practiced today, in churches across the world, in a form that would be recognizable to a Christian from the fifth century. But most Westerners have never encountered it. What follows is not a pitch. It is a history — the history of what Christians believed before the denominations existed.

The First Christians

Christianity began as a Jewish movement. The first followers of Jesus were Jews who continued to attend the synagogue and the Temple. What set them apart was a set of claims about Jesus of Nazareth: that He was the Messiah, that He had risen from the dead, and that His resurrection had changed the fundamental terms of human existence.

The earliest Christian worship was simple: the breaking of bread (the Eucharist), the reading of Scripture, baptism of new converts, and communal prayer. By the end of the first century, the basic shape of Christian worship was established — a liturgy of the Word (readings and preaching) followed by a liturgy of the Eucharist (bread and wine). This structure has remained essentially unchanged in the Eastern churches for two thousand years.

The Apostle Paul's letters, written in the 50s AD, reveal a community already wrestling with deep theological questions: the relationship between Jewish law and Christian faith, the nature of the resurrection, the meaning of baptism, the role of spiritual gifts. These were not simple people with simple beliefs. They were thinkers engaged in a high-stakes intellectual project — figuring out the implications of what they claimed had happened.

The Age of Persecution

For roughly three centuries, Christianity was illegal in the Roman Empire. Christians were periodically arrested, tortured, and executed. The persecutions varied in intensity — sometimes local, sometimes empire-wide — but the threat was constant.

This shaped the faith profoundly. Martyrdom became the highest form of witness (the word "martyr" literally means "witness" in Greek). The blood of the martyrs was understood not as a tragedy but as a seed — each death a public testimony that these people believed their claims about Christ so completely that they would die rather than deny them.

The early Church also developed during this period a remarkable intellectual tradition. The Church Fathers — figures like Ignatius of Antioch, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus of Lyon, and Clement of Alexandria — wrote extensively, engaging with Greek philosophy, refuting heresies, and articulating what Christians believed and why. Their writings are dense, sophisticated, and frequently brilliant.

I am the wheat of God, and am ground by the teeth of wild beasts, that I may be found the pure bread of Christ.
— Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Romans, c. 110 AD (written en route to his execution)

Nicaea: When Doctrine Was Hammered Out

In 313, Emperor Constantine legalized Christianity. In 325, he convened the first Ecumenical Council at Nicaea. This is where the history gets interesting — and where most popular accounts get it wrong.

The common story is that Constantine "invented" Christianity at Nicaea, or that the Council decided which books would be in the Bible, or that it "made" Jesus divine by vote. None of this is true. The Council of Nicaea addressed a specific theological crisis: the teaching of Arius, a priest from Alexandria, who argued that the Son of God was a created being — the first and greatest of God's creatures, but not God in the same way the Father is God.

The debate was fierce. Arius was articulate, popular, and had significant episcopal support. The opposing position, championed by a young deacon named Athanasius, held that the Son is "of one essence" (homoousios) with the Father — not a creature but God from God, light from light, true God from true God.

Athanasius won the argument. The Nicene Creed — still recited every Sunday in Eastern churches — was the result. But Athanasius did not win by executive authority or political power. He won by argument. He spent decades defending his position, was exiled five times, and outlasted multiple emperors who tried to suppress his teaching.

The stakes of the Arian controversy were not academic. If the Son is a creature — even the highest creature — then the Incarnation does not bridge the gap between God and humanity. A creature cannot deify other creatures. Only God can make humans participants in the divine life. If Christ is not fully God, then the entire Eastern understanding of salvation (theosis — humanity becoming by grace what God is by nature) collapses.

Athanasius saw this with absolute clarity. His famous formula — "God became human so that humans might become God" — only works if both halves are real. If God did not truly become human (if the Son is a lesser being), then humanity has not truly been united to God. The entire project of salvation, as the East understood it, depended on the full divinity of Christ.

This is why the Nicene Creed spends so many words on the identity of the Son. It is not theological nitpicking. It is the load-bearing wall of the entire structure.

The Seven Councils

Nicaea was the first of seven Ecumenical Councils — gatherings of bishops from across the Christian world that defined the boundaries of orthodox belief. They span from 325 to 787 AD, and they addressed questions that, if you set aside the theological vocabulary, remain deeply human.

Who is Jesus? Fully God and fully human — not half of each, not God pretending to be human, not a human adopted by God. Two complete natures in one Person, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation" (Council of Chalcedon, 451).

Who is the Holy Spirit? Not a force or a power but a Person — fully God, "who proceeds from the Father," worthy of worship equal to the Father and the Son (Council of Constantinople, 381).

Can you make images of Christ? Yes — because He took on a human face. To deny that Christ can be depicted is to deny that He truly became human (Council of Nicaea II, 787).

The process was not smooth. Councils contradicted each other. Emperors intervened. Bishops were exiled, recalled, exiled again. Some councils were later declared invalid. The whole thing was, frankly, a mess — a glorious, human, contentious mess from which emerged a body of doctrine that has remained unchanged for over a thousand years.

What is remarkable is the method. The Councils operated not by papal decree (the Bishop of Rome was one voice among many, honored but not commanding) and not by democratic vote. They operated by consensus — a painful, sometimes decades-long process of arguing, testing, and refining until the Church as a whole recognized that a particular formulation expressed the faith accurately. The Eastern term for this is sobornost: conciliarity. The truth is discerned by the community, not dictated by an individual.

What They Believed About Salvation

Here is where ancient Christianity diverges most sharply from what most modern Westerners expect.

The dominant Western understanding of salvation — the one most English-speaking people encounter — is essentially legal. Humanity sinned. Sin incurs a penalty. Christ paid the penalty on the cross. If you accept this payment (by faith, by baptism, by some combination), the penalty is remitted. You are declared righteous. You go to heaven when you die.

The ancient Church did not think this way. Not because it rejected law and justice, but because it operated from a different root metaphor. Salvation was understood primarily as healing.

Humanity was sick. The sickness was death — not just physical death, but a deep distortion of human nature, a turning away from God that left us fragmented, enslaved to passions, cut off from the source of life. Christ came not to pay a debt but to cure the patient. The Incarnation — God becoming human — was the medicine. By uniting divine nature to human nature in His own Person, Christ healed human nature from the inside.

What has not been assumed has not been healed.
— St. Gregory the Theologian, 4th century

Gregory's axiom is one of the most important sentences in the history of theology. It means: Christ had to take on every aspect of human nature — body, soul, mind, will, emotion — in order to heal every aspect of human nature. If He skipped any part, that part remains broken. This is why the early Church fought so hard against anyone who denied the completeness of Christ's humanity or divinity. Both had to be real and complete for the cure to work.

The goal of salvation, in this framework, is not merely to avoid punishment. It is theosis — becoming by grace what God is by nature. Sharing in the divine life. Being so saturated with God's presence that you radiate His qualities: love, peace, wisdom, creativity, compassion. Not losing your personhood but having it completed, brought to its full potential.

This is what ancient Christians actually believed about the point of being alive.

▾

The Western doctrine of original sin, shaped largely by Augustine of Hippo, holds that all humans inherit the guilt of Adam's sin. We are born guilty, deserving of punishment, and only God's grace can remedy this inherited legal problem.

The Eastern tradition reads the same biblical texts differently. In the East, the term is "ancestral sin" (propatorikon amartima). Adam's sin introduced death into human nature, and all humans inherit the consequences of that corruption — mortality, weakness, a tendency toward sin. But we do not inherit Adam's guilt. An infant is not guilty of anything. The problem is not legal but medical: we are born into a sick world, and the sickness is in us from the start.

This distinction matters enormously. In the Western framework, the central problem is guilt, and the solution is forgiveness. In the Eastern framework, the central problem is death, and the solution is resurrection — new life, healed nature, participation in the divine.

The Church Before the Splits

For a thousand years, there was one Church, and it looked remarkably similar across a vast geography — from Ireland to India, from Ethiopia to Scandinavia. Worship was liturgical (structured, ancient, sacramental). Authority was conciliar (councils, not a single leader). Theology was patristic (rooted in the writings of the Church Fathers). Spirituality was ascetic (fasting, prayer, self-discipline as the basic framework of Christian life).

It was not perfect. It was frequently messy, political, and human. But it held together in a way that nothing since has replicated.

The Great Schism of 1054 — when the Latin West and the Greek East formally split — is usually given as the date this unity ended. The reality is more gradual. The Western and Eastern halves of Christianity had been growing apart for centuries: linguistically (Latin vs. Greek), politically (Rome vs. Constantinople), and theologically (different emphases on law, grace, authority, and the role of the bishop of Rome). The split, when it came, was not a clean break but a slow tearing.

What emerged in the West after 1054 — Scholasticism, the Reformation, the Counter-Reformation, evangelical Protestantism — developed without the Eastern tradition's input or influence. The Christianity most English-speaking people know is a product of this Western development. It is, historically speaking, a branch — not the trunk.

Still Here

The ancient tradition did not disappear. It is still practiced today by roughly 300 million people worldwide — in Orthodox churches (Greek, Russian, Romanian, Antiochian, and many others) and in Eastern Catholic churches (which share the same theology and worship but are in communion with Rome). The liturgy celebrated this Sunday in an Orthodox or Eastern Catholic parish would be recognizable to a Christian from the sixth century. The hymns are the same. The prayers are the same. The theology is the same.

This is not nostalgia or historical re-enactment. It is a living tradition that considers itself in unbroken continuity with the apostolic Church. Whether that claim is justified is a question for the reader. What is not debatable is that the tradition exists, that it predates every modern denomination by at least a thousand years, and that it offers a version of Christianity that is radically different from what most Westerners assume the faith to be.

It has no altar calls. No prosperity gospel. No culture wars. No argument about the age of the Earth. It has, instead, a liturgy of extraordinary beauty, a theology of stunning intellectual depth, and a spiritual tradition that has been refining its understanding of the human encounter with God for two millennia.

If that surprises you, you are not alone. Most people in the West have simply never been told.
